**Day 90 — April 1, 2026**

The morning after the storm brought crystalline air and the particular quiet that follows violence. Sefiathan woke to find the gathering transformed—tents flattened or torn, supplies scattered, the careful order of their camp reduced to chaos that would take days to restore. But people were already moving through the wreckage with purposeful calm, assessing damage, helping neighbors, beginning the work of rebuilding without drama or complaint.

He emerged from his tent—miraculously intact thanks to Korrin's stake work—and immediately spotted a family struggling with a collapsed shelter. Their tent poles had snapped in the wind, canvas now a sodden heap on the ground. A woman was trying to untangle the mess while two children huddled nearby, looking cold and overwhelmed.

Sefiathan walked over without thinking about it. "Need help?"

The woman looked up, relief washing across her face. "We've never dealt with damage this bad. I don't even know where to start."

"Let's start with getting you somewhere dry." Sefiathan turned to scan the gathering, spotted Maren's supply tent still standing sturdy. "Maren will have space. Come on."

He led them over, helped them get settled in Maren's covered area, made sure they had hot tea and blankets. The whole time, something in his chest felt tight. Not anxiety. Something else. Recognition, maybe. Of a pattern he'd been living his whole life without seeing it clearly.

Always protecting others. Always making sure everyone else was okay. Always taking responsibility for other people's comfort, other people's needs, other people's disasters. Moving through the world as if his primary function was to absorb impact, to shield others from difficulty, to make sure they never had to face hard things alone.

But who was protecting him?

The question arrived with uncomfortable clarity as he watched the family settle into warmth and safety. He'd spent the last hour making sure they were okay. Had anyone checked if he was okay? Had anyone asked if his tent had survived, if he needed anything, if the storm had frightened him?

No. Because he was the one who protected. The one who showed up for others. The one whose needs were never quite as urgent as everyone else's.

Someone showed you their true colors and it stung. The words from the reading echoed in his mind. But their betrayal says nothing about your value. Stop replaying what you should have seen. Start planning what you'll do next.

He thought about his father. About the years of showing Sefiathan's sensitivity as weakness, dismissing his struggles, making it clear that Sefiathan's emotional needs were inconvenient complications rather than legitimate requirements. About how Sefiathan had absorbed that message and turned it into a way of moving through the world—always putting others first, always making sure everyone else was comfortable, always treating his own needs as optional extras that could be indefinitely deferred.

That was a betrayal, wasn't it? His father showing him clearly that Sefiathan's value lay in being useful, being uncomplicated, being easy to ignore. And Sefiathan had spent twenty-seven years replaying that message, trying to figure out what he'd done wrong, how he could have earned proper care, what he needed to fix about himself to become worth protecting.

But he'd been asking the wrong questions. The betrayal said nothing about his value. It said something about his father's limitations. About the kind of love his father was capable of offering. About what his father believed children were for.

And Sefiathan could keep replaying it, keep trying to solve it, keep treating it like a puzzle with a solution that would make everything make sense. Or he could start planning what he'd do next. How he'd live differently. How he'd stop protecting everyone else while leaving himself completely exposed.

Calantha found him an hour later, helping another family salvage their scattered supplies. She watched him work for a moment before speaking.

"You're doing it again."

"Doing what?"

"Taking care of everyone else. Making sure everyone's okay. Moving through this disaster like it's your job to fix it all." She paused. "Has anyone asked if you're okay?"

The question landed like a physical blow. "I'm fine."

"That's not what I asked." Calantha's voice was gentle but firm. "I asked if anyone's checked on you. If anyone's made sure you're warm and dry and not overwhelmed by all this."

Sefiathan felt something crack in his chest. "No."

"Right. Because you're the protector. The one who shows up for others. The one whose needs are never quite urgent enough to matter." She sat down on a partially collapsed crate. "You know what you're worth protecting too?"

"I know that—"

"No, I don't think you do. I think you spent so long not being protected by the people who should have protected you that you decided your value lay in protecting others. That you could earn care by making sure everyone else never had to earn it from you. But that's just another version of the same betrayal, Sefiathan. Just you doing to yourself what your father did to you."

The words hit with brutal accuracy. He thought about his childhood. About his mother protecting him, yes, but his father treating his sensitivity like a character flaw. About learning that his needs were burdensome, his feelings were excessive, his struggles were self-indulgent complications in an otherwise functional household. About deciding that if he couldn't be easy, he could at least be useful. Could at least make sure everyone else felt safe and seen even if he never did.

"I don't know how to stop," he admitted quietly. "Protecting others is just what I do. It's automatic."

"I know. But you're allowed to make demands too." Calantha gestured around the destroyed gathering. "You're allowed to say 'I need help' or 'I'm overwhelmed' or 'Someone check on me because I'm not okay.' You're allowed to require care instead of just providing it."

Make demands. The phrase felt foreign, almost aggressive. Sefiathan had spent his life trying not to be demanding. Trying to be low-maintenance. Trying to need as little as possible because needing things made him burdensome and burdensome people got left behind.

But he was allowed to make demands. Allowed to require care. Allowed to say 'I'm worth protecting too' without apologizing for the imposition.

Korrin appeared from around a damaged tent, spotted them, and walked over with his usual lack of urgency. "Your tent survived."

"Yeah. Thanks for the stake work."

"Did you eat this morning?"

The question surprised Sefiathan. "What?"

"Did you eat. You've been running around helping everyone for hours. Did anyone make sure you had breakfast?" Korrin's expression was matter-of-fact. "Because you're worth protecting too, you know. Worth someone making sure you're fed and warm and not running yourself into the ground trying to fix everyone else's disasters."

Sefiathan felt his throat tighten. "I'm fine."

"That's what you always say. But 'fine' isn't the same as 'cared for.'" Korrin pulled something wrapped in cloth from his pack. "Here. Bread and cheese from before the storm. Eat it."

It was such a small thing. But Sefiathan felt it anyway—the care in someone checking on him, making sure he was okay, demanding that he take care of himself because his wellbeing mattered to them. Not because he'd earned it through service. Just because he existed and that was enough.

He took the food. Ate it slowly while Calantha and Korrin sat with him in comfortable silence. Let himself be protected for once instead of always being the protector.

Stop replaying what you should have seen. Start planning what you'll do next.

What he'd do next was start making demands. Start requiring care instead of just offering it. Start believing that his value didn't lie in protecting others while leaving himself perpetually exposed.

He was worth protecting too.

The storm had shown him that much.
