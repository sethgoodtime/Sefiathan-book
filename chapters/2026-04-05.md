**Day 94 — April 5, 2026**

The fourth day of waiting felt different. Sefiathan woke before dawn with a clarity that bypassed thought entirely—his body knew what to do before his mind could interfere with questions or calculations. He dressed in the dark, moved through the silent gathering, walked straight to the edge of the Frostmere without hesitation.

The ice had changed overnight. Not visibly. But the resonance underneath had shifted from diffuse awareness to focused attention. Whatever was waking beneath the Thornspire wasn't just stirring anymore. It was preparing to surface. And every instinct Sefiathan had built through three months of disappointment and recalibration was telling him exactly what that meant.

It was time to move.

Not away from it. Toward it.

He stood at the ice edge watching light begin to touch the frozen marshlands and felt the certainty settle in his bones. This wasn't gambling. This was recognition. The setbacks of the last three months—every moment he'd felt inadequate, every time he'd questioned his instincts, every disappointment that had forced him to recalibrate—those hadn't been failures. They'd been rehearsals. Building the knowledge that now told him with absolute conviction what needed to happen next.

He needed to go to the mountains. Alone. Meet whatever was waking directly instead of waiting for it to come to the gathering.

The knowledge felt dangerous. Reckless even. But it didn't feel like gambling because he'd already put in the work. Had already spent months learning to trust his instincts, to recognize when his body knew things his mind couldn't yet articulate, to move toward difficulty instead of calculating safe distances from it. This was just the logical extension of everything he'd been practicing. The calculated move that only looked like risk to people who hadn't done the rehearsals.

Behind him, footsteps crunched on frost. Orvenna's voice came quiet but unsurprised. "You're going."

"Yes."

"Alone?"

"Yes."

She moved beside him, her weathered face turned toward the distant peaks. "The others will think you're being reckless. That you're gambling with something you don't understand."

"I know." Sefiathan felt the truth of it without defensiveness. "But I'm not gambling. I've spent three months learning to read this. Learning when to trust what I feel versus what I think I should feel. This isn't impulse. It's instinct sharpened by disappointment."

"The disappointments taught you," Orvenna said. It wasn't a question. "Every time you got it wrong, every time your instincts led you somewhere uncomfortable, every setback that made you question yourself—those were just rehearsals. Building the knowledge you're using right now."

Sefiathan nodded slowly. He thought about the last three months. The ankle injury that had forced him to stay still when he wanted to keep moving. The emotional weight that had crashed over him in waves when he'd expected linear progress. The moments of doubt and fear and uncertainty that had made him question whether he knew anything at all. Every single one of those experiences had taught him something about himself, about his patterns, about when to trust his gut and when his gut was just repeating old fears.

And now, standing at the ice edge with something vast and ancient focusing its attention on him from beneath the mountains, all of that accumulated knowledge was speaking with one voice: Go. Meet it directly. This is your calculated move.

"When will you leave?" Orvenna asked.

"Now. Before anyone can try to talk me out of it."

"Good." She turned to face him fully. "You understand what you're walking toward?"

"No. But I understand that I need to walk toward it anyway. That what looks like risk to others is actually the move I've been preparing for since I left Millhaven." He paused. "The setbacks weren't failures. They were calibration. Making sure I could tell the difference between real instinct and fear disguised as intuition."

"And you can tell the difference now?"

"Yes." The certainty in his own voice surprised him. "I can feel when fear is trying to dress itself up as wisdom. When my mind is trying to protect me from something that doesn't actually need protection. This isn't that. This is recognition. Something under those mountains is waiting for me specifically. And I need to go meet it before it comes looking."

Orvenna's expression held something like approval. "Trust that instinct. The knowledge you've accumulated through disappointment has sharpened your ability to read situations accurately. What you're feeling isn't recklessness. It's precision."

She reached into her pack and pulled out a leather-wrapped bundle. "Supplies. Enough for a week. And this—" she handed him a small carved stone "—is a wind-anchor. If you need help, hold it and speak into the wind. I'll hear you."

Sefiathan took both offerings, feeling the weight of Orvenna's trust. She wasn't trying to stop him. Wasn't questioning his judgment or demanding he explain his reasoning. She was trusting that he knew what he was doing because she'd watched him build the knowledge that made this move calculated rather than reckless.

"Thank you."

"Don't thank me. Just make it. Whatever's waiting for you under those mountains, meet it on your terms. Trust the instincts you've been sharpening for three months." She turned to go, then paused. "And Sefiathan? The others may think you're gambling. Let them. You know you're not. That's enough."

She disappeared back into the gathering as dawn light spread across the frozen marshlands. Sefiathan stood alone at the ice edge, feeling the pull from beneath the mountains intensify. Feeling his body already knowing the path even as his mind tried to catalog everything that could go wrong.

But he'd learned something crucial over the last three months: setbacks weren't failures. They were rehearsals. And he'd run enough rehearsals to trust what his instincts were telling him now. The knowledge he'd accumulated through disappointment had taught him to distinguish between fear and wisdom, between impulse and precision, between gambling and making calculated moves that only looked risky to people who hadn't done the work.

He started walking. North across the frozen marshlands, toward the Thornspire peaks, toward whatever vast and ancient thing was focusing its attention on him with increasing intensity. Each step felt inevitable. Not because he'd planned this route. But because his body had known it before his mind caught up. The rehearsals had prepared him. The disappointments had sharpened his instincts. The setbacks had taught him to tell the difference between real risk and fear wearing wisdom's clothes.

This wasn't gambling. This was the calculated move.

Behind him, the gathering would be waking soon. Korrin and Calantha would realize he was gone. Others might think he'd done something reckless, impulsive, dangerous. Let them. He wasn't moving on their assessment of risk. He was moving on instincts that had been sharpened by three months of getting things wrong until he learned to get them right.

The ice crunched under his boots. The wind carried whispers of something vast stirring beneath stone. The pull in his chest intensified with each step north. And Sefiathan walked toward it without hesitation, trusting the knowledge that disappointment had built in him—that what looked like gambling to others was actually his most carefully calculated move yet.

The setbacks had taught him. The disappointments had sharpened him. The rehearsals had prepared him.

Now it was time to make it.
