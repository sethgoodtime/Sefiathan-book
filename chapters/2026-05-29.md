# **Day 148 — May 29, 2026**

The problem with finally being direct about what he wanted was that Sefiathan kept expecting immediate results. He'd spent weeks being polite with Orvenna, scaling back his questions, waiting for permission. Then he'd finally demanded real teaching and she'd started explaining the resonance—how his attention was a catalyst, how he woke up potential in others, how his odd sideways love reminded things how to be fully themselves.

But apparently understanding what you were didn't automatically mean knowing how to work with it. Orvenna had been teaching him for eight days now and Sefiathan was still mostly just sitting still, listening to underground currents, trying to notice when the resonance was happening. It felt frustratingly similar to what he'd been doing before—except now he understood what he was building toward, which somehow made the slow pace more aggravating rather than less.

This morning she'd had him sit beside a dying evergreen at the edge of the gathering. The tree was maybe thirty years old, needles browning from the inside out, bark splitting where it shouldn't. Some kind of root disease, Orvenna said. Not salvageable through normal means. But she wanted him to sit with it anyway. Pay attention to it. See if he could feel the difference between his attention and his attention-with-resonance.

"They're not the same thing," she'd explained. "You've been doing both your whole life but treating them like they're identical. Regular attention is just noticing. Resonance happens when you notice and care in that specific way you have. When you stop shrinking yourself to make others comfortable and just be fully present with your odd angles."

So Sefiathan had been sitting with the tree for two hours now. Paying attention. Noticing the pattern of dead needles, the way sap had stopped flowing properly, the sense of slow collapse. Trying to care in the specific way Orvenna meant. Trying to be authentically weird at a dying tree.

It felt performative. Like he was doing a bad impression of himself. The harder he tried to access his genuine oddness the more self-conscious he became, which defeated the entire purpose. How were you supposed to be authentic on command? How did you stop shrinking when the act of trying to stop shrinking was itself a kind of performance?

He gave up around mid-morning and went to find Korrin. His friend was working with the young centaurs again, teaching them about tracking. Sefiathan waited until they broke for lunch before approaching.

"I think I'm doing it wrong," he said without preamble.

"Doing what wrong?"

"Being myself. Orvenna wants me to stop shrinking to make others comfortable. To just be fully present with all my odd angles and unconventional ideas. But every time I try, it feels like I'm performing authenticity instead of actually being authentic."

Korrin considered this while chewing a piece of dried meat. "Maybe stop trying?"

"That's not helpful."

"No, I mean—you're treating being yourself like it's a skill you have to master. Like there's a correct way to be weird and you haven't figured out the technique yet." Korrin gestured broadly at the gathering around them. "But you've been being yourself the whole time you've been here. That weird intense thing you do where you pay too much attention to everything? That's you being yourself. You don't have to manufacture it."

The observation landed uncomfortably. Sefiathan had been treating authenticity like a switch he needed to find and flip. Like his genuine oddness was somewhere inside him that he could access if he just tried hard enough. But that was just another version of shrinking—trying to perform being unshrunk rather than actually relaxing into his natural shape.

"I think I'm self-conscious about it now," he admitted. "Before Orvenna explained the resonance, I was just being weird without thinking about it. But now that I know my attention does something, I'm trying to do it right. Trying to be weird in the specific way that catalyzes potential in others. Which makes me performative instead of genuine."

"So stop trying to do it right," Korrin said. "Just go back to being weird without thinking about it. Trust that your attention will do whatever it does without you trying to control it."

The advice was frustratingly simple and probably correct. Sefiathan had spent his whole life being odd in ways that made people uncomfortable. Had built his melody through noticing things sideways, had furnished his internal room with specific preferences, had learned to occupy space without apologizing. All of that had been genuine because he hadn't been trying to perform authenticity—he'd just been letting himself exist in his natural shape.

But now he was self-conscious. Now he was aware his oddness had power. And that awareness was making him treat his authentic weirdness like something that needed to be managed, controlled, deployed strategically. Which was just shrinking with extra steps.

He went back to the dying tree after lunch. Sat down in the same spot but this time didn't try to access his resonance or be authentically odd or notice in the specific way Orvenna wanted. Just sat. Just looked at the tree. Just let himself be bored and frustrated and self-conscious without trying to fix any of it.

The tree was dying in a way that felt almost peaceful. Not dramatic or violent but just gradually ceasing. The disease was in the roots but the needles were what showed it—browning from the inside out, still green at the tips where the tree kept trying. There was something stubborn about it. Something that reminded him of his father actually—continuing through momentum even after the core had given up.

That was an odd thought. Probably not useful. Sefiathan almost pushed it away but then remembered what Korrin had said about just being weird without trying to control it. So he let the association sit. Let himself notice that the tree's slow collapse felt familiar. That watching something die from its foundation while maintaining appearances felt like watching his own childhood house—his father still building furniture while the family dynamics rotted underneath, everyone performing normalcy while the core was sick.

The recognition didn't feel profound. Just true. And uncomfortable in a way that made him want to stop paying attention, to think about something less personal, to redirect his focus somewhere safer.

But that was shrinking. That was making himself comfortable by avoiding his actual thoughts. That was performing polished normalcy instead of accepting his authentic weirdness included thinking odd things, making uncomfortable associations, noticing patterns that weren't always pleasant.

So he kept sitting with it. Kept noticing that the tree reminded him of his childhood. Kept feeling the discomfort of that recognition without trying to make it mean something or turn it into insight or perform understanding for an imaginary audience. Just let himself be a person who thought weird things while looking at a dying tree.

Something shifted. Not dramatically. Not with any sense of power or magic or intentional resonance. Just a subtle change in how he was sitting—less performance, less trying, more actually present with his own odd attention. His thoughts were still uncomfortable. The tree was still dying. But he wasn't shrinking himself to make any of it more palatable.

The afternoon passed that way. Sefiathan sat and thought odd thoughts and let himself be authentically weird without trying to weaponize it into catalyst work or prove he was being genuine. Just existed in his natural shape. Just stopped making himself comfortable for an audience that didn't exist.

When Orvenna came to check on him near evening, she crouched beside the tree and touched its bark with her weathered hand. "Hm."

"I didn't do anything," Sefiathan said.

"You stopped performing." She showed him where she was touching—a small section of bark that looked subtly different from the surrounding areas. Less split. Less diseased. "The tree remembers how to be healthy here. Where you were paying attention without trying to control what your attention did. Without shrinking to make yourself comfortable."

"But I was just thinking about my father."

"I didn't say you had to think appropriate thoughts. I said you had to stop shrinking." Orvenna stood, brushed bark dust from her hands. "Being yourself is exhilarating because it's uncomfortable. Your odd angles and unconventional ideas make people notice for all the right reasons. But you have to actually be willing to be uncomfortable. Can't perform authenticity. Can't try to be weird in acceptable ways. Just have to exist in your natural shape and trust that your genuine oddness is exactly what's needed."

The people worth knowing would appreciate his authentic weirdness over polished fakeness. He'd been learning that all year. But apparently he'd started treating his weirdness like something that needed to be strategic, managed, deployed correctly. Had been trying to be authentic in ways that made himself comfortable rather than just being uncomfortable and trusting that was okay.

Worth working on. Worth learning that being yourself meant being actually yourself—odd thoughts, uncomfortable associations, genuine reactions—not performing the version of yourself you thought would be most effective.

The evening was cooling and the tree was still dying but there was a small patch of bark that remembered health and Sefiathan had stopped trying so hard.
