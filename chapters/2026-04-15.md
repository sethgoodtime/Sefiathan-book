**Day 104 — April 15, 2026**

Ten days into the mountains and Sefiathan's supplies were running low. Not dangerously low—Orvenna had packed well—but low enough that he'd started rationing without consciously deciding to. The leather pouch of dried meat that should have lasted another week had already dwindled to three days' worth. The cheese was gone. He had bread left, some nuts, water from snowmelt that he boiled each night over carefully managed fires.

He could turn back. Should turn back, probably, according to any reasonable assessment of the situation. He was deep in the Thornspire now, in territory that saw maybe one human traveler a year, following nothing but instinct and the pull in his chest that had grown from a whisper to a constant hum. The gathering was eight days behind him. Help was unreachable. And he was running out of food.

But the pull was stronger than hunger. Stronger than the reasonable voice suggesting he retreat to safety. So he kept walking north, toward whatever vast thing waited beneath the ice, trusting that his body knew what his mind couldn't yet articulate.

The mountains had changed him. Or maybe revealed him. Ten days of solitude stripped of all social performance, all attempts to manage how others perceived him, all the careful calibration he'd learned at the gathering. Out here, there was no one to protect, no one to prove anything to, no one whose comfort he needed to prioritize over his own survival. Just him and the mountain and the pull getting stronger with each step north.

He'd thought a lot about money during those ten days. Not because he needed it—what use was coin in the Thornspire?—but because walking alone through snow gave him too much time to think, and his mind kept circling back to the same uncomfortable question: What was he actually worth?

Not in some abstract spiritual sense. In concrete terms. What value did he bring? What could he offer? What made him worth anyone's time or attention or care?

The question felt dangerous because it kept trying to reduce him to transactions. To measurable outputs. To some quantifiable return on investment. His father would have understood that question. His father had always treated relationships like ledgers—what you gave versus what you got back, whether someone was worth the trouble they caused, whether investing time in Sefiathan would yield useful results.

But Sefiathan had spent months learning that he wasn't a transaction. That his value didn't lie in what he could do for others. That needing things wasn't weakness. That making demands was legitimate. All of that work, all of that growth, and yet out here in the mountains with his supplies running low and his body getting tired, the old questions kept surfacing: What are you actually worth? What value do you bring? Why should anyone care if you make it back or not?

He stopped for the night in a small cave carved into the mountainside by centuries of wind. Built a fire from scraps of wood he'd carried from lower elevations. Rationed out his evening meal—less than his body wanted, more than true desperation required. Sat watching flames while snow fell outside and thought about worth.

The thing about being raised to see yourself as a burden was that you internalized a very specific kind of math. You learned to calculate your value based on how little trouble you caused, how useful you made yourself, how successfully you could anticipate and meet others' needs before they had to ask. You learned that your worth was always conditional, always dependent on performance, always something that could be revoked if you failed to justify the space you took up.

And Sefiathan had spent twenty-seven years running those calculations. Trying to prove he was worth keeping around. Trying to demonstrate enough value to justify his existence. Treating himself like an investment that needed to show returns or risk being abandoned.

But what if that whole framework was wrong? What if worth wasn't something you had to prove or justify or demonstrate? What if you just had it, inherently, regardless of what you could offer anyone else?

The thought felt radical. Almost offensive to the part of him that still measured his value in transactions. But sitting alone in a cave in the Thornspire with no one to perform for and nothing to prove, Sefiathan felt something shift. A recognition that he'd been asking the wrong question all along.

The question wasn't "What am I worth?" The question was "What do I actually want?"

Not what should he want. Not what would make him valuable to others. Not what would justify the space he took up. Just what did he actually want, for himself, regardless of whether it made him useful or easy or worth anyone's investment?

He wanted to finish this journey. Wanted to meet whatever was pulling him north with such insistence. Wanted to see what happened when he stopped calculating worth and just moved toward what called him. Wanted to trust that his value existed independent of what he could offer anyone else.

The fire crackled. Snow fell silent outside the cave. And Sefiathan sat with the uncomfortable truth that he'd spent months learning to make demands and stop apologizing and take up space, but he'd never actually stopped measuring his worth in terms of transactions. He'd just gotten better at demanding fair ones. Better at recognizing when he was giving more than he received. Better at setting boundaries around his time and energy.

But he'd never actually questioned whether his value needed to be measured at all.

The pull in his chest intensified. That ancient attention focusing on him with increasing precision. Not because of what he could offer it. Not because he'd proven himself worthy of its notice. Just because he existed and that was enough. Just because he'd walked toward it instead of away and that mattered regardless of any return on investment.

He thought about his father. About the way his father had always treated Sefiathan's sensitivity as a liability, his emotions as inconvenient complications, his struggles as failures of character that made him less valuable than he should be. His father had taught him to measure worth in outputs. In usefulness. In whether you justified the trouble you caused.

But his father had been wrong. Not wrong about whether Sefiathan struggled—he did. Not wrong about whether Sefiathan was sensitive—he was. Wrong about whether any of that affected his value. Wrong about whether worth needed to be proven rather than simply recognized.

Sefiathan was worth caring about not because he brought measurable value to others. Not because he made himself useful enough to justify investment. Not because he successfully performed adequacy. He was worth caring about because he existed. Because he was trying. Because he was willing to walk toward difficult things instead of away from them. That was enough. That had always been enough.

The revelation felt too simple to be true. But simple didn't mean easy. Simple meant recognizing that all the complex calculations he'd been running for twenty-seven years—all the attempts to prove his worth, to demonstrate his value, to justify the space he took up—had been answers to a question that was fundamentally flawed.

He didn't need to prove what he was worth. He just needed to know what he actually wanted and trust that wanting it was justification enough.

What he wanted was to keep walking north. To meet whatever waited beneath the ice. To see what happened when he stopped calculating and just showed up fully. His supplies were low but not gone. His body was tired but still strong. The pull was getting stronger. That was enough information. The rest was just fear dressed up as practical concerns.

He banked the fire and settled into his bedroll, feeling the certainty in his bones. Tomorrow he'd keep walking north. Not because he could prove it was the right choice. Not because he could demonstrate sufficient value to justify the risk. Just because he wanted to. Because the wanting itself had worth independent of outcomes or returns or anyone else's assessment of whether he was making a sound investment.

The question wasn't "What am I worth?"

The question was "What do I actually want?"

And the answer was simple: to keep walking toward what called him, trusting that his value existed whether he could measure it or not.

That was enough.
