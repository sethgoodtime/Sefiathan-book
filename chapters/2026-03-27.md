**Day 85 — March 27, 2026**

Sefiathan woke before dawn with the uncomfortable realization that he'd been treating himself like a project that needed fixing. Every flaw catalogued. Every misstep analyzed. Every perceived failure turned into evidence that he was fundamentally malfunctioning in ways that required constant correction.

He lay in his bedroll listening to the pre-dawn sounds of the gathering—someone coughing in a nearby tent, the wind moving through bare branches, the crackle of an early fire being built—and felt the weight of it. The endless appraisal. The brutal honesty he'd been so proud of turned inward until it became something corrosive. A machine checking itself for defects, finding them everywhere, convinced that proper functioning required eliminating every imperfection.

But he wasn't a machine. Wasn't something that could be debugged into optimal performance. He was just a person, messy and uncertain and sometimes contradictory, and the flaws he'd been cataloguing so meticulously weren't malfunctions. They were just parts of being human that he'd decided were unacceptable.

The Co-Star wisdom arrived sideways: the flaws you fixate on wouldn't even register to someone who loves you. Stop the endless appraisal. You are not a machine malfunctioning.

Sefiathan sat up slowly, his body stiff from another night on frozen ground. Twenty-five days at the Frostmere gathering now. Long enough that he'd stopped thinking about it as temporary, started thinking about it as just where he was. Long enough that the rhythm of this place had become familiar—the wind-listening sessions with Orvenna, meals around shared fires, the careful dance of proximity and solitude that gatherings required.

Long enough that his patterns had solidified here too. The self-criticism. The constant monitoring of his own performance in interactions. The mental ledger of every awkward moment, every word that came out wrong, every time he'd been too much or not enough or just fundamentally off in ways that confirmed his suspicion that he was somehow defective.

He thought about yesterday's clarity about loving people who didn't love him back. About his father's consistent inability to see him clearly. About Tavis's withdrawal when Sefiathan had drawn a boundary. About all the relationships where he'd poured warmth into voids and called it connection.

But underneath all of that was this other thing. This constant appraisal of himself. The way he treated every flaw like evidence of fundamental brokenness. The way he examined himself with a harshness he'd never turn on anyone else. The way he'd convinced himself that brutal honesty about his failings was somehow noble, necessary, the only way to improve.

Except it wasn't improving him. It was just making him tired. Making him treat himself like a problem to be solved rather than a person to be known.

He dressed and stepped out into air sharp enough to sting his lungs. The gathering was barely stirring. A few people moved between tents in the grey pre-dawn light, breath visible in clouds. Sefiathan made his way to the communal fire area and started building up the coals from last night, adding kindling with the careful attention that fire required.

Orvenna appeared out of the dimness like she always did—suddenly just there, as if she'd condensed out of the cold air itself.

"You're up early," she observed, settling onto a log across from him.

"Couldn't sleep. Kept thinking about flaws." Sefiathan fed a slightly larger stick to the growing flames. "How much energy I spend cataloguing them. Trying to fix them. Treating myself like something broken that needs constant correction."

"And how's that working for you?"

"It's not. It's just exhausting." He sat back, letting the fire establish itself. "I keep thinking if I can just identify all the defects, all the ways I'm not functioning properly, I can fix them. Become the version of myself that's acceptable. That works right. That doesn't need so much correction."

Orvenna made a sound that might have been amusement or acknowledgment. "You know what someone who loves you would see when they look at you?"

"What?"

"Not the catalogue of flaws you've memorized. Just you. Moving through the world in your particular way. Struggling sometimes. Succeeding sometimes. Being awkward and uncertain and occasionally getting it wrong. Being human." She paused. "The flaws you fixate on wouldn't even register to them. Because they're not looking at you like you're a machine that needs debugging."

Sefiathan felt that land somewhere deep. He thought about the people who actually cared about him. His mother. Korrin. Calantha. When they looked at him, were they seeing the catalogue of defects he carried? The list of ways he was too sensitive, too intense, too complicated, too much work? Or were they just seeing him—messy and uncertain and trying his best—and finding that acceptable exactly as it was?

"I don't know how to look at myself that way," he admitted. "Without the constant appraisal. Without tracking every flaw and failure. It feels irresponsible. Like if I'm not constantly monitoring for defects, I'll just get worse."

"You'll just be human," Orvenna corrected. "Which you already are, whether you approve of it or not."

More people were emerging now, drawn by the fire. Calantha appeared with her usual lack of ceremony, settling beside Sefiathan with a nod of greeting. She held her hands toward the flames without speaking, content in the early morning quiet.

Sefiathan watched the fire build. Thought about all the mental energy he spent on self-criticism. The way he replayed conversations looking for every awkward moment, every wrong word, every indication that he'd performed inadequately. The way he treated normal human uncertainty like evidence of fundamental malfunction. The way he'd convinced himself that someone worthwhile wouldn't struggle this much with basic social interaction, with knowing what he needed, with trusting his own perceptions.

But Korrin struggled too. So did Calantha. So did literally everyone he'd met on this journey who'd been honest about their internal experience. The difference was they didn't treat their struggles like character defects that needed elimination. They just moved through them and kept going, treating difficulty as part of being alive rather than proof of being broken.

"I think I've been confusing brutal honesty with brutal kindness," Sefiathan said quietly, more to himself than to Orvenna or Calantha. "Looking at myself with brutal honesty just meant cataloguing every flaw. But brutal kindness would be looking at the same person and seeing someone who's trying his best in legitimately difficult circumstances."

"There you go," Calantha said, her first words of the morning. "That's the shift. Not pretending the flaws don't exist. Just recognizing they're not evidence of being fundamentally defective. They're just part of being a person."

Sefiathan sat with that. With the difference between acknowledging a flaw and treating it like proof of malfunction. Between noticing he'd been awkward in a conversation and adding it to a mental list of ways he was socially defective. Between struggling with something and interpreting the struggle as confirmation that he was broken in ways other people weren't.

Someone who loved him wouldn't see him that way. Wouldn't track his failures with the meticulous attention he brought to self-criticism. Wouldn't interpret every struggle as evidence of fundamental inadequacy. They'd just see him—complicated and sometimes difficult and occasionally getting it spectacularly wrong—and love him anyway because the flaws weren't the point. The person was the point.

He thought about his mother. How she'd never made his sensitivity into a character defect. Had just accepted that he felt things deeply and needed space to process and sometimes withdrew when overwhelmed. Had loved him through all of it without requiring him to fix those things about himself. Had treated them as just how he was rather than problems requiring correction.

That was brutal kindness. Seeing clearly without making everything into evidence of malfunction.

The fire crackled and grew. More people gathered around it, nursing morning tea, slowly waking into the day. Sefiathan watched them move through early morning routines without judgment, without cataloguing their awkwardness or tracking their flaws. Just seeing them as people doing their best.

He could do that for himself too. Could look at his struggles and complications without treating them like malfunctions. Could acknowledge flaws without building cases for his own inadequacy. Could be human without requiring himself to be a perfectly functioning machine.

The endless appraisal was just another wall. Another way of keeping warmth at distance. Another way of treating himself like something that needed fixing before he deserved care.

But he wasn't a machine malfunctioning. He was just a person learning how to be gentler with his own messy, uncertain humanity.

That seemed like enough.
