# **Day 168 — June 18, 2026**

The expedition to the Wandering Dunes had left Kethril three days ago and Sefiathan was learning that travel with scholars was different from travel with friends. Not worse, just different. More purposeful. Less room for wandering attention. Everyone had roles and responsibilities and expectations about how quickly they should cover ground. It was efficient in ways that made him slightly uncomfortable—like watching a well-oiled machine when he was used to organic meandering.

Korrin had come with him, along with Calantha who'd decided her fire magic might be useful in desert conditions. Tavis had stayed at the Frostmere, finally settled enough to stop preparing speeches for every interaction. The expedition leader was a woman named Thessaly—not the fae he'd met at the Singing Stones but a human scholar with the same name, which had caused brief confusion when introductions happened. This Thessaly was maybe fifty, weathered by years of fieldwork, efficient with words in ways that suggested she had no patience for unnecessary talking.

The other expedition members were mostly academics. People who studied ancient architecture or lost civilizations or magical theory. They were kind enough but treated Sefiathan with careful curiosity—like something interesting but potentially fragile. The wind-reader with unusual resonance abilities. The odd one Orvenna had recommended. They asked him questions about how his attention worked and he tried to explain but kept feeling like he was performing understanding instead of just existing in his strangeness.

That was the thing he kept noticing on this trip. How much easier it had been to just be himself at the Frostmere where everyone was already weird. Where his oddness was expected and even valued. Out here with scholars who had normal magic and normal ways of thinking, he found himself falling back into old patterns. Apologizing for taking up space. Making himself smaller. Trying to be useful in conventional ways instead of trusting his genuine weirdness.

This morning they'd stopped to rest in the shade of some desert rocks and one of the younger scholars had started talking about her research. Something about how ancient civilizations built resonance chambers to amplify magical workings. She was excited about the theory, gesturing enthusiastically while she explained harmonic frequencies and architectural acoustics. Sefiathan had found himself genuinely interested but when she'd asked what he thought, his first instinct had been to deflect. To say something vaguely supportive but non-committal. To not risk having opinions that might be wrong.

He'd caught himself mid-deflection and stopped. Actually thought about what she was asking. Actually noticed that he had thoughts about resonance and how attention might interact with architectural intention. Actually risked saying something that might be stupid.

"I think attention is its own kind of architecture," he'd said slowly. "Like how you build spaces changes what can happen in them, but so does how you pay attention to spaces. Maybe the ancient resonance chambers worked because people were building with intention that matched the attention they'd bring later."

The young scholar had paused, considered this with genuine interest rather than polite tolerance. "That's actually consistent with some of the theories about sympathetic construction. The idea that buildings can hold intentional patterns if they're made with focused awareness."

It was such a small thing. Just sharing an actual thought instead of performing supportiveness. Just risking having opinions instead of deflecting to avoid being wrong. But it felt significant in ways Sefiathan was still processing. Like practicing a skill Orvenna had been teaching him—directing his attention with intention instead of scattering it everywhere to avoid committing to anything specific.

The scholar whose name he'd learned was Maren—different Maren from the Crossroads Post but similarly direct—had joined the conversation then. Asking him more about how his resonance worked. Whether he'd noticed patterns in what his attention woke. Whether there were conditions that made the effect stronger or weaker.

And Sefiathan had answered honestly instead of apologetically. Had shared observations without prefacing them with disclaimers about probably being wrong. Had let himself exist as someone who knew things about his own weird magic instead of constantly deferring to people with more conventional knowledge.

It was exhausting in new ways. Not the exhaustion of performance but the exhaustion of actually showing up. Of being present with his full odd self instead of the carefully edited version. Of trusting that what he noticed mattered even when it was strange.

They'd started moving again as the afternoon heat began to ease. The Wandering Dunes were still several days south but the landscape was already shifting. Less rock, more sand. Fewer plants, more open sky. Something about the emptiness made Sefiathan's attention want to scatter—like his notice-everything tendency was trying to fill the void with anything to focus on.

Calantha walked beside him for a while, comfortable silence between them. She'd been different since talking with her friend—less performed, more genuinely herself. Still careful but not constantly bracing. They'd developed a kind of friendship that didn't require constant maintenance. Just occasional presence. Just showing up for each other without keeping score.

"You're doing that thing again," she said after a while.

"What thing?"

"Where you make yourself smaller. Like you're trying not to take up too much space in the group."

The observation landed with uncomfortable accuracy. He had been doing that. Hanging back slightly. Not quite joining conversations unless directly asked. Apologizing with his posture even when no one had suggested he was doing anything wrong.

"It's easier at the Frostmere," he admitted. "Everyone there is already weird. Here I feel like I'm supposed to be normal and useful and I don't know how to do both at once."

"Maybe you're not supposed to do both." Calantha adjusted her pack, squinting at the horizon. "Maybe being actually useful means being weird instead of trying to make yourself palatable to people who probably want your actual strangeness anyway."

That was the thing Sefiathan kept forgetting. The expedition had asked for him specifically because of his unusual abilities. Because his odd attention woke sleeping things. Because his resonance worked in ways conventional magic didn't. They wanted the weirdness. They'd invited the strangeness. And here he was trying to minimize it, trying to make himself easier to accommodate, trying to be helpful in normal ways instead of trusting that his genuine oddness was exactly what they needed.

"Your heart is a battlefield," Calantha said quietly.

"What?"

"Something my friend told me. About how I keep fighting battles with people who aren't actually fighting me. Keep defending against attacks that aren't coming. Keep treating every interaction like I need to protect myself from hurt that already happened instead of hurt that's actually present."

Sefiathan understood immediately. That was exactly what he was doing. Fighting defensive battles against past dismissiveness. Protecting himself from his father's old judgment even though his father wasn't here. Bracing against Mira's rejection even though these were different people offering different things. Treating every scholar's curiosity like it might secretly be mockery even though they'd shown him nothing but genuine interest.

"How do you stop?" he asked.

"I'm still figuring that out. But I think it starts with noticing who's actually reaching for you now instead of who pushed you away before."

They walked in silence after that. The sand was getting deeper, harder to walk through. Sefiathan's boots kept slipping slightly with each step. The sun was lowering toward the horizon, painting everything in shades of amber and rust. Something about the light made the landscape feel liminal—caught between day and night, between solid ground and shifting sand.

Maren found him when they stopped to make camp. Asked if he'd be willing to practice his resonance on some stone samples she'd brought from previous digs. Just to see if his attention affected older materials differently than living things like trees. She was genuinely curious, not testing him, not judging whether he was competent enough. Just interested in what his weird magic could do.

Sefiathan's first instinct was to apologize for probably not being able to help. To warn her his abilities were unpredictable. To protect himself from disappointing her by managing expectations down to nothing. But he caught himself. Actually noticed he was fighting a battle that wasn't happening. Defending against criticism that wasn't coming. Treating her genuine interest like it might secretly be judgment.

"I'll try," he said instead. Simple. Not apologizing. Not deflecting. Just agreeing to show up with his actual strange self and see what happened.

They sat together as the evening cooled. Maren laid out three stone samples—pieces of carved rock from different ancient sites. Sefiathan touched each one carefully, paying attention with his odd sideways focus. Not performing. Not trying to force results. Just letting his genuine notice-everything tendency settle on the stones and see what woke.

The first one did nothing. The second one warmed slightly under his fingers—subtle but real. The third one started humming. Not audibly but he could feel it—resonance building in the stone like it was remembering something. Remembering being part of something larger. Remembering purpose.

Maren's eyes went wide. "I've never seen that before. Never heard of any magic that could wake dormant resonance in completely inactive materials."

"Is that what I'm doing?"

"I don't know. But whatever it is, it matters." She touched the humming stone carefully, feeling the vibration. "The ruins we're traveling to—they've been dead for centuries. No active magic, no residual power. Just empty architecture. But if your attention can remind things how to resonate again..."

She didn't finish the thought but Sefiathan understood. Maybe his weirdness wasn't something to minimize or apologize for. Maybe it was exactly what the expedition needed. Maybe the people reaching for him now actually wanted his strangeness instead of some performed normal version.

The battle was in his heart, not in the world. Fighting defenses against past hurt instead of being present with current possibility. The scholars weren't his father. This expedition wasn't Mira leaving. These stones weren't evidence of inadequacy. Just opportunities to show up fully odd and see what woke.

The stone kept humming as the stars came out and Sefiathan sat with the recognition that maybe he could stop fighting battles that had already ended.
