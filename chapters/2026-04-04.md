**Day 93 — April 4, 2026**

Sefiathan woke to Korrin shaking his shoulder, voice urgent but controlled. "Get up. Orvenna wants everyone at the listening stones. Now."

The sky was barely grey with dawn. Sefiathan dressed quickly, his body moving on autopilot while his mind tried to catch up. Around him, the entire gathering was stirring—people emerging from tents, moving toward the stones with the same purposeful urgency. Something had shifted in the night. Something in the wind.

By the time he reached the clearing, at least forty people had gathered. Orvenna stood at the center of the listening stones, her weathered face turned toward the north with an expression Sefiathan had never seen before. Not quite fear. Something deeper. Recognition of something vast approaching.

"The deep wind changed three hours ago," she said without preamble. "Something is moving under the Thornspire Mountains. Something old. The resonance has been building for weeks but last night it crystallized into direction. Into purpose." She paused, scanning the gathered faces. "Whatever's been sleeping under the ice is waking up."

A ripple of unease moved through the crowd. Sefiathan felt it in his chest—that pull he'd been sensing since he first arrived at the Frostmere. That ancient attention turning toward him. Toward all of them.

"What do we do?" someone asked.

"We prepare," Orvenna said flatly. "Close the laptops. Put away the spreadsheets. Whatever contingency plans you've been making, whatever analysis you've been running—set it aside. This isn't something you can calculate your way through. The moment demands bare feet on grass."

Sefiathan felt those words land with uncomfortable accuracy. He thought about the last three months. The careful way he'd been moving through this journey. The constant mental checking, the endless appraisal of whether he was doing it right, whether he was prepared enough, whether he'd accounted for all the risks. The way he'd been treating his own growth like a project with measurable milestones instead of just living it.

He'd spent months preparing. Analyzing. Calculating every risk. Building frameworks for understanding himself, for navigating relationships, for moving through the world with more intention. The mental work had served its purpose. But standing here with the wind carrying warnings of something vast and ancient stirring beneath the mountains, all that preparation suddenly felt irrelevant.

The moment demanded bare feet on grass. Not another contingency plan.

"How much time do we have?" Calantha asked from beside him.

"Days. Maybe a week." Orvenna's voice was matter-of-fact. "Whatever's waking won't arrive here immediately. But it's coming. And when it does, you'll either be ready to meet it directly or you won't."

The gathering dispersed slowly, people returning to their camps with the weight of Orvenna's words settling over them. Sefiathan found himself walking toward the edge of the Frostmere, toward the frozen marshlands that stretched toward the Thornspire peaks in the distance. Korrin and Calantha followed without speaking, the three of them moving in the comfortable silence that came from months of proximity.

They stopped at the ice edge. The marshlands looked deceptively peaceful in the early morning light—frozen reeds catching sun, ice gleaming, everything still and quiet. But underneath, Sefiathan could feel it. That resonance. That pull. Getting stronger by the hour.

"You feel it more than the rest of us," Korrin observed. "Whatever's under there. It's paying attention to you specifically."

Sefiathan nodded slowly. "It has been for weeks. I thought I was imagining it at first. That ancient awareness turning toward me every time I listened to the deep wind with Orvenna. But it's real, isn't it?"

"Very real." Calantha's voice was quiet. "The question is what you're going to do about it."

What was he going to do about it? His first instinct was to prepare. To analyze. To build contingency plans for every possible outcome. To spend the next week calculating risks and trying to anticipate what meeting something ancient and powerful might require of him.

But that was just more spreadsheets. More laptop work. More trying to control something that couldn't be controlled through careful planning.

The plans he'd made were solid enough. Three months of internal work. Three months of learning to stop apologizing for existing, to take up space, to trust his own perceptions, to make demands, to recognize his value. Three months of building a foundation for moving through the world differently. That preparation was done. Complete. Served its purpose.

Now he needed to close the laptop. Put his bare feet on grass. Meet whatever was coming with direct presence instead of more analysis.

"I'm going to stop preparing," Sefiathan said. The words felt strange in his mouth. Foreign. Like admitting defeat. "I'm going to stop trying to anticipate and calculate and build frameworks. I'm just going to be here. Present. Ready to meet it directly."

"Good." Korrin's approval was matter-of-fact. "Because whatever's waking up under those mountains isn't interested in your contingency plans. It's interested in you. In what you actually are, not what you think you should be."

Sefiathan felt that truth settle. He'd spent the last three months learning about himself. Understanding his patterns. Building awareness of his tendencies and triggers and ways of moving through the world. All of that had been necessary. Had created the foundation he needed. But at some point, preparation had to end and action had to begin.

At some point, you had to put your bare feet on grass and just be present with what was actually happening.

They walked back to camp slowly. Around them, the gathering was alive with nervous energy. People securing supplies, reinforcing tents, making plans for evacuation if needed. Everyone preparing. Everyone analyzing risks. Everyone trying to feel like they had some control over what was coming.

Sefiathan understood the impulse. But he also recognized it for what it was—another form of avoidance. Another way of staying in the comfortable space of planning instead of stepping into the uncomfortable space of direct encounter.

He thought about his tendency to overthink. To analyze every interaction until he'd stripped it of spontaneity. To prepare so thoroughly for conversations that he lost track of what he actually wanted to say. To treat every situation like it could be solved through enough mental work. That tendency had protected him in some ways. Had helped him make sense of complicated patterns. But it had also kept him at a distance from direct experience.

The moment demanded something different. Demanded presence without all the mental scaffolding. Demanded showing up exactly as he was, with whatever preparation he'd already done, and trusting that to be enough.

Calantha caught his elbow as they neared the tents. "You're doing that thing again."

"What thing?"

"Overthinking. I can see you building frameworks for how to not overthink." Her smile was wry. "Just stop. Whatever's coming, you're ready for it. Or you're not. But more analysis won't change that."

She was right. He'd spent months preparing. The work was done. The spreadsheets had served their purpose. Now he needed to close the laptop and trust that the person he'd become through all that internal work could meet whatever was coming.

That night, lying in his tent listening to wind move through the gathering, Sefiathan practiced letting go. Not of awareness. Not of everything he'd learned. Just of the constant need to analyze and prepare and calculate. He practiced being present with the pull he felt from beneath the mountains. That ancient attention focusing on him with increasing clarity.

He didn't try to figure out what it meant or what it wanted or how he should respond. He just felt it. Let himself be aware of it without needing to understand it. Trusted that when the moment came, he'd know what to do.

The plans he'd made were solid enough. The person he'd become was ready enough. The moment demanded bare feet on grass. Not another contingency plan.

And for the first time in months, Sefiathan felt genuinely prepared. Not because he'd analyzed every risk. But because he'd stopped trying to. Because he trusted the work he'd already done. Because he was ready to meet whatever was coming with direct presence instead of more preparation.

Something vast was waking beneath the ice. And Sefiathan would meet it barefoot, wild, without apology.

That was preparation enough.
