# **Day 125 — May 6, 2026**

The conversation with Korrin happened on the fourth morning back at the gathering. Sefiathan had been avoiding it—not deliberately, but through the natural busyness of returning. Wendell and Maeve needed to prepare for their journey home. Orvenna had students to catch up with. Other wind-readers wanted to know what he'd found in the mountains, what had called him that far north, whether the recognition had revealed anything about the fractures appearing across Parogar.

He'd answered their questions carefully. Described the pull, the impossibly deep wound in the ice, the darkness singing his melody back with variations that felt older than the mountains. But he'd left out the part about nearly dying. Left out the six days without food, the hypothermia, the moment when he'd understood his body was failing and felt relief that the limitation was external rather than some failure of his worth. Those details felt private. Not because he was ashamed but because they belonged to a different conversation. The one he needed to have with Korrin.

He found him near the edge of the gathering where the ice gave way to frozen marsh. Korrin was alone, which was unusual—the centaur typically surrounded himself with people, drawn to community the way Sefiathan was drawn to solitude. But this morning he stood by himself, staring out toward the Thornspire peaks with an expression Sefiathan couldn't read from a distance.

The approach felt impossible. Every step toward his friend carried weight that had nothing to do with the physical distance. This wasn't about apologizing for existing or for following his instincts. This was about owning that his careless certainty had cost someone who cared about him. That disappearing for a month without explanation had consequences beyond his own experience. That being right about needing to go didn't justify the way he'd gone.

Korrin heard him coming—centaur hearing was sharp enough to catch footsteps on frozen ground from thirty feet away—but didn't turn. Just kept staring at the mountains like they'd personally offended him.

"I thought you were dead," Korrin said finally. His voice was flat. Carefully controlled. "For two weeks I thought you'd died alone up there and I'd never even know where to look for your body."

Sefiathan stopped a few feet away. Gave Korrin space to keep not looking at him. "I'm sorry."

"For what?" Now Korrin did turn, and his expression was harder than Sefiathan had ever seen it. "For going? For not telling me you were going? For making me spend two weeks imagining finding you frozen in some crevasse? Be specific."

The anger was justified. Sefiathan could feel that clearly—not as criticism but as consequence. He'd earned this. Had damaged something that mattered through careless pursuit of his own certainty. The challenge was owning it without falling back into the old patterns. Without apologizing for existing. Without treating his need to follow the call as something that required justification.

"I'm sorry I didn't tell you I was leaving," he said. Kept his voice steady. "Sorry I made you worry. Sorry I disappeared for a month without giving you any way to know I was alive. That was careless. You deserved better than that."

"I deserved—" Korrin's voice cracked. He looked away again, toward the mountains. "You nearly died. Orvenna told me. Six days without food. Hypothermia. If they'd been a day later finding you—" He stopped. Took a breath that wasn't quite steady. "And I'm angry at you for that. For being so reckless. But I also feel guilty for being angry because obviously you went through something terrible and I should just be relieved you're alive. Except I am angry. And I don't know how to hold both things at once."

The honesty landed like gift. Korrin wasn't hiding behind polite forgiveness or pretending the hurt wasn't real. He was offering the actual complexity of his response—the anger and the worry and the guilt all tangled together. Which meant he trusted Sefiathan enough to show him the mess instead of the cleaned-up version.

"You can be both," Sefiathan said quietly. "Angry and relieved. They're not mutually exclusive."

"Are they not?" Korrin's laugh was bitter. "Because it feels pretty fucking exclusive to me. It feels like I should pick one—either be angry that you disappeared without a word or be grateful you survived. But I can't seem to manage either one cleanly."

Sefiathan watched his friend struggle with the contradictions and recognized something familiar. The same either-or thinking he'd been working to untangle all year. The belief that feelings had to be coherent, that you couldn't hold opposing truths simultaneously, that if one thing was true then its opposite couldn't also be real. But that wasn't how feelings worked. They existed in layers. In tangles. In combinations that didn't resolve into simple clarity.

"What if both are true?" he offered. "You're angry I was careless and relieved I survived. Those aren't competing—they're just different responses to the same situation. Your anger doesn't negate your relief. Your relief doesn't invalidate your anger. They're both real."

Korrin was quiet for a long moment. Then: "That voice in my head kept saying I had no right to be angry. That you were the one who nearly died, so my feelings about it didn't matter. That I should just be grateful Orvenna found you in time and stop making it about me."

"That voice is wrong," Sefiathan said. It came out more certain than he'd expected. "Your experience matters. What my choices cost you—the worry, the fear, the two weeks of not knowing—that was real. That happened. Me nearly dying doesn't erase what you went through. Both things can be true at once."

He was teaching himself as much as Korrin. Learning that impact existed independent of intent. That he could be right about needing to follow the call and still account for what his careless pursuit had cost someone who cared whether he survived. That relationships required holding multiple truths simultaneously—that his journey mattered and that Korrin's worry mattered, that his instincts were valid and that his friend's anger was justified, that he could take up space and still need to consider the space others occupied.

The chains were made of smoke. The old voice that said his needs didn't matter, that he had to minimize his impact, that existing fully meant being a burden—that voice was just one possibility his mind manufactured. But the new voice that said his truth mattered more than other people's feelings, that being right justified any pursuit, that taking up space meant not having to account for relationships—that voice was smoke too. Both extremes were limitations masquerading as freedom.

The actual freedom was in the middle. In the complexity. In holding that his instincts mattered and so did the people who cared about him. In following his truth while still maintaining the tact that made being heard possible. In taking up space while considering the space others occupied. In being right without powering through everyone's legitimate concerns.

"I should have told you I was leaving," Sefiathan said again. "Not to ask permission. Not to justify the journey. Just because you're my friend and friends tell each other when they're about to disappear into mountains for a month. That's baseline consideration. And I didn't offer it because I was so certain about following the pull that I didn't think about anything else. That was careless. That hurt you. I'm sorry for that specifically."

Korrin finally looked at him fully. The anger was still there but softer now. Mixed with something that looked like relief. "You really almost died."

"I did."

"And you'd do it again."

It wasn't a question but Sefiathan answered anyway. "Yes. But with better preparation. With proper supplies. With telling people where I was going. I was right about needing to follow the call. But I was wrong about how I followed it. Those are both true."

"Multiple truths," Korrin said, and his voice carried something almost like humor. "You're learning."

"Slowly."

They stood together in silence for a while. The gathering was waking up around them—voices carrying across the frozen marsh, fires being built, the ordinary sounds of people starting their day. The Thornspire peaks stood distant and impassive, holding whatever vast thing had called Sefiathan north. He hadn't completed the descent. Hadn't reached the bottom. But he'd arrived at the threshold and that had been enough. The recognition had held. The ancient attention had seen him clearly. And now he was back among people who cared whether he survived, learning that certainty without tact was just isolation wearing a different name.

Worth working on. Worth the discomfort of owning his impact. Worth maintaining the relationships that made survival possible.

His mind was powerful enough to limit his life. Was already manufacturing new chains to replace the old ones. But he could notice the thoughts and watch them drift away. The smoke had no substance. He could step through it. Could hold multiple truths without needing them to resolve into simple clarity. Could be right and still listen. Could follow his instincts and still account for other people. Could take up space while considering whose space he was taking up.

Listening cap. Punchlines. Show not tell. The wisdom wasn't in choosing one. It was in knowing when each mattered.
