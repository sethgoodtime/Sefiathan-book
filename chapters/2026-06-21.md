# **Day 171 — June 21, 2026**

The ruins appeared on the horizon just after dawn—jagged stone teeth rising from sand that had buried most of their original height. Sefiathan had expected something more dramatic, more obviously significant, but these looked like broken rocks. Just weathered stone that time and wind had reduced to suggestions of what they'd been. The scholars were excited anyway, already pulling out tools and notebooks, discussing angles and architectural features he couldn't identify from this distance.

He'd spent the past two nights thinking about what Maren had said after the stone started humming. About how his attention could wake dormant things. About how maybe the ruins needed exactly his kind of strangeness. But underneath the excitement was something uncomfortable—this awareness that the expedition needed one person to be dominant but he didn't know if it should be him.

The standoff wasn't obvious. No one was arguing about leadership or control. Scholar Thessaly ran the expedition with efficient authority and no one questioned her decisions about routes or rest stops or how to approach the site. But there was this other thing happening—this undefined space around who would take charge of the actual magical investigation. Who would direct the work once they reached the ruins. Who would make decisions about how to proceed when ancient architecture met unusual resonance.

Thessaly had brought him because his magic was different, potentially more effective with dormant materials. But she was still the expedition leader. Still the one with decades of experience and academic authority. And Sefiathan kept feeling this pull between deferring to her expertise and trusting his own instincts. Between following established protocols for investigating ancient sites and letting his odd attention work however it wanted to work.

His silence about it wasn't patience. That was the uncomfortable recognition that had settled in his chest last night. He'd been telling himself he was being respectful, thoughtful, giving Thessaly space to lead. But actually he was just afraid. Afraid of taking up too much space. Afraid of asserting himself and being wrong. Afraid that if he suggested something different from standard procedure, everyone would realize he didn't actually know what he was doing and shouldn't be here.

The endless circling was exhausting. This constant internal negotiation about whether to speak up or stay quiet. Whether to trust his instincts or defer to conventional wisdom. Whether to be dominant about his own weird magic or let others tell him how to use it. It drained both of them—him and Thessaly—because she kept waiting for him to take initiative with the resonance work while he kept waiting for her to give him permission.

Korrin found him standing slightly apart from the group as they prepared to approach the ruins. "You're doing it again."

"What?"

"Standing on the edges. Waiting for someone to tell you it's okay to be central."

The observation landed sharp and accurate. Sefiathan had positioned himself away from the main cluster of scholars, not quite part of the planning conversation but close enough to hear if someone asked him something. Ready to be useful if called upon but not presuming to take up space in the decision-making.

"I don't know if I should be the one leading this," he admitted.

"No one's asking you to lead the whole expedition. But your magic is why we're here. Your attention is what might wake these ruins. That's your domain. That's where you get to be dominant."

The word felt too strong. Too aggressive. Sefiathan didn't want to dominate anything or anyone. He just wanted to help, to be useful, to contribute without causing problems. But maybe that was the issue. Maybe making everything about not causing problems was its own kind of problem. Maybe his silence wasn't respectful patience but just fear dressed up as consideration.

Thessaly called the group together and outlined the approach plan. They'd spend today surveying the visible structures, taking measurements, documenting the current state. Tomorrow they'd begin more active investigation—testing for residual magic, attempting to activate any dormant systems, seeing what could be learned about the site's original purpose.

She looked at Sefiathan when she talked about testing for magic. "We'll want your resonance work front and center tomorrow. You'll need to decide how you want to approach it. What you need from us to support your process."

It was an invitation to take charge. To be dominant about his own domain. To stop waiting for permission and just claim the space his magic required. But Sefiathan's first instinct was to deflect. To say something vague about being happy to help however she thought best. To put the decision back on her so he didn't have to risk being wrong about what his attention needed.

He caught himself mid-deflection. Actually noticed he was about to choose fear over clarity. About to stay silent because speaking up felt too vulnerable. About to let someone else make decisions about his own magic because taking charge felt too aggressive.

"I'll need to work alone at first," he said, voice steadier than he felt. "My attention works better without an audience. Without feeling like I'm performing or trying to get specific results. I need to just sit with the stones and let the resonance happen naturally."

Thessaly nodded, making notes. "How long do you typically need?"

"I don't know yet. Depends on how dead the ruins actually are. But probably hours, not minutes. And I'll need people to trust the process even if it looks like nothing's happening."

"Can you explain what you're doing so we can document it properly?"

"No." The word came out more firmly than he'd intended but he didn't take it back. "I can describe what I notice afterward but explaining while I'm doing it disrupts the attention. Makes it more about performance than genuine noticing."

This was calculated risk. Clear self-advocacy instead of accommodating whatever people wanted from him. Setting boundaries instead of making himself endlessly flexible. Being dominant about his own weird magic even though it might inconvenience others or deviate from standard research protocols.

Thessaly studied him for a moment, something assessing in her expression. Then she smiled slightly. "Good. I was wondering if you'd figure out you needed to take charge of your own domain."

The relief was immediate and uncomfortable. She'd been waiting for him to claim space. Had probably been frustrated by his constant deferring. Had invited him specifically because his magic was different but he'd been trying to make it conform to conventional approaches instead of trusting its genuine strangeness.

The endless circling had been draining them both. His fear making him silent. Her uncertainty about how to lead someone whose process she didn't understand. Both of them stuck in this standoff about who would take charge while the actual work waited for someone to just make a move.

They spent the day surveying and Sefiathan tried to practice being present with his authority. When scholars asked him questions about his resonance, he answered directly instead of apologetically. When someone suggested a conventional magical testing method that wouldn't work with his attention, he said so clearly instead of just going along to avoid conflict. When Maren asked if he wanted to examine some carved stones they'd found, he said yes because he genuinely wanted to, not because refusing might disappoint her.

It was exhausting in new ways. Not the exhaustion of performance but the exhaustion of actually taking up space. Of being dominant about his own domain. Of trusting that his magic mattered enough to claim room for it even when that meant saying no to other approaches.

Calantha found him in the late afternoon, sitting in the shade of a partially buried wall. "You're different today."

"How?"

"Less apologetic. More just... present. Like you've decided you're allowed to be here doing what you're doing."

That was exactly what he'd decided. Or was trying to decide. Was practicing deciding. The standoff had been entirely internal—his fear fighting his authority, his silence fighting his voice, his need to defer fighting his need to take charge. No one else had been keeping him from being dominant about his own magic. He'd been doing that himself. Circling endlessly in fear while pretending it was patience or respect or consideration.

The ruins rose dark against the sunset and tomorrow he'd sit with them properly. Would let his odd attention work without explaining or justifying or performing understanding. Would be dominant about his own domain because that was the only way forward. Would make his move instead of waiting for permission that no one else could give.

The battle had been in his heart and maybe the work was just noticing that and choosing to stop fighting.
