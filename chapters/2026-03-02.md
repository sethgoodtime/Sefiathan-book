**Day 60 — March 1, 2026**

The morning brought sharp clarity wrapped in uncomfortable truth: Sefiathan had been loving people who didn't love him back. Not in the dramatic, unrequited romance way. In the quieter, more corrosive way of constantly making excuses for their absence, reading meaning into their silence, convincing himself that care was present even when all evidence suggested otherwise.

He'd been doing it with his father for years. Making excuses for the dismissiveness, the way conversations always pivoted away from Sefiathan's struggles toward more practical matters. Telling himself that his father just expressed love differently, that he cared even if he didn't show it, that Sefiathan was too sensitive to need verbal affirmation of his worth.

But yesterday's realization about walls and warmth had cracked something open. He'd been letting warmth through to people who actually wanted to receive it—Calantha, Korrin, even that woman at the fire. But he'd also been pouring warmth into voids. Into people who consistently didn't reciprocate. Into relationships where he was always the one reaching out, adjusting, making space for their limitations while his own needs went unmet.

The Co-Star reading—though he'd never name it that—hit sideways: stop reading meaning into their silence. Stop making excuses for their absence. The right person won't leave you guessing whether you matter to them.

Sefiathan sat with his morning tea, the gathering waking around him in slow increments. He thought about his family. How he'd spent twenty-seven years trying to earn his father's approval through different versions of himself. How he'd made excuses for his father's consistent inability to see him clearly, to ask about his life, to show genuine interest in who Sefiathan actually was beyond his usefulness or lack thereof.

"You matter to him, he just doesn't know how to show it." How many times had his mother said some variation of that? How many times had Sefiathan repeated it to himself when his father's indifference cut particularly deep? But what did it mean to matter to someone who never showed it? Who never made space for you in their life beyond obligatory interactions? Who left you constantly guessing whether you were worth their time, their attention, their care?

That wasn't love. That was just... proximity. Biology. The accident of being born into the same house.

The thought felt almost blasphemous. You were supposed to love your family. Supposed to make excuses for their failures to show up properly. Supposed to accept whatever crumbs of affection they offered and be grateful for them. But Sefiathan was tired of crumbs. Tired of convincing himself that people cared when their actions consistently suggested otherwise.

He spotted Tavis across the gathering, helping someone adjust their tent stakes. They hadn't spoken since their confrontation two days ago. Sefiathan felt a familiar impulse rise—to go over there, to smooth things over, to explain his harshness or apologize for being too direct. To make it okay again even if it meant backpedaling on the truth he'd spoken.

But he caught himself. Recognized the pattern. This was another version of it—making excuses for someone's absence, trying to fill the void of their withdrawal with his own effort, his own reaching out, his own need to fix what they'd chosen to leave broken.

Tavis had walked away. Had chosen to interpret Sefiathan's honesty as cruelty rather than care. Had made it clear through his absence that he wasn't interested in the kind of friendship that included difficult truths. And Sefiathan had been sitting here for two days treating that absence like something temporary, something that could be resolved if he just found the right words to bring Tavis back.

But Tavis had shown him clearly who he was. Someone who needed to be handled carefully. Who couldn't tolerate hard feedback. Who would withdraw affection when Sefiathan stopped performing helpfulness. The right person won't leave you guessing whether you matter to them. Tavis had made it abundantly clear: Sefiathan mattered as long as he was useful. As long as he was willing to fix things. As long as he didn't challenge the helplessness.

The moment he'd drawn a boundary, Tavis had disappeared. That was the answer. Not silence that needed interpretation. Not absence that needed excuses. Just a clear statement of what Sefiathan's worth was in Tavis's eyes: conditional on compliance.

Sefiathan felt something hard settle in his chest. Not anger. Something colder. Recognition.

He'd been doing this his whole life. Loving people who didn't love him back. Not because they were cruel or deliberately withholding, but because they simply didn't have the capacity or desire to love him in the way he needed. And instead of accepting that and adjusting accordingly, he'd spent years making excuses for them. Convincing himself their silence meant something other than disinterest. Convincing himself their absence was about their struggles rather than their priorities.

His father wasn't going to suddenly develop emotional intelligence. Wasn't going to wake up one day and ask Sefiathan about his journey with genuine curiosity. Wasn't going to stop being dismissive of struggles that didn't fit into his practical worldview. That was just who his father was. Sefiathan could keep making excuses for it—he's from a different generation, he doesn't know how to express feelings, he shows love through actions not words—or he could accept that his father's version of love, whatever it was, left Sefiathan feeling unseen and unvalued. And that was real. That mattered. That wasn't something Sefiathan needed to explain away or minimize.

Calantha appeared beside him, settling onto the log with her usual lack of ceremony. "You look like you're grieving something."

"I think I am." Sefiathan stared into his tea. "I'm realizing how much energy I've spent loving people who don't love me back. Not in obvious ways. In quiet ways. Where I'm always the one reaching out, making excuses for their absence, convincing myself they care even when they consistently show me they don't."

"That's a hard thing to see clearly." Calantha's voice was gentle. "Easier to keep making excuses. To blame yourself for not being patient enough or understanding enough. To believe that if you just loved them better, they'd love you back properly."

"But they won't." The words tasted bitter. "My father won't suddenly become interested in who I am. Tavis won't suddenly develop the capacity to hear hard truths. The people who leave you guessing whether you matter to them—they're giving you the answer. You just have to stop making excuses for it."

Calantha nodded slowly. "So what do you do with that? Just stop loving them?"

"I don't know." Sefiathan felt the uncertainty of that admission. "Maybe. Or maybe just stop waiting for them to love me back. Stop making their inability to show up properly into something I need to fix or accommodate or explain away. Stop treating their silence like a mystery that needs solving instead of just accepting it as the answer."

They sat in silence for a while. Sefiathan watched the gathering move through morning routines. Watched Tavis laugh with someone across the clearing, apparently unbothered by their estrangement. Watched Korrin haul water with methodical care. Watched Orvenna emerge from her tent and move toward the wind-listening stones with her particular economy of motion.

The right person won't leave you guessing whether you matter to them. The words settled with uncomfortable finality. He'd spent so much of his life guessing. Trying to interpret silence. Making excuses for absence. Convincing himself that love was present even when he felt consistently unseen, unvalued, uncertain of his place in someone's life.

But people who genuinely cared didn't make you guess. They showed up. They reached out. They made space for you in ways that felt clear and consistent. They didn't leave you constantly wondering if you were too much or not enough or fundamentally worth their time.

His mother did that. Showed up even when Sefiathan was prickly or withdrawn. Reached out even when he didn't respond immediately. Made it clear through consistent action that he mattered to her even when he was being difficult. That was love. Clear and present and unwavering.

His father didn't do that. Never had. Sefiathan had spent twenty-seven years making excuses for the absence of that clarity. Telling himself it meant something other than what it obviously meant: that his father's version of care, whatever it was, didn't include genuine interest in Sefiathan's inner life. Didn't include making space for struggles that weren't immediately fixable. Didn't include the kind of seeing that Sefiathan had needed his whole life.

And that hurt. Grieved something. The loss of the father he'd wished his father could be. The death of the hope that someday it would be different, that someday his father would suddenly see him clearly and care about what he saw.

But acknowledging the hurt was better than continuing to make excuses for it. Better than spending more years convincing himself that silence meant something other than absence. That someone who consistently made him guess his worth was actually showing love in some coded way that just needed proper translation.

Sefiathan finished his tea. Stood. Looked at Calantha with something like resolve settling in his chest.

"I'm done making excuses for people who don't show up," he said. "Done loving people who leave me guessing. Done treating their silence like something I need to interpret instead of just accepting it as the answer."

Calantha smiled. "Good. Now the hard part: actually doing that. Actually walking away from people you love who don't love you back. Actually stopping the excuses when every instinct wants to give them one more chance."

Sefiathan nodded. She was right. Seeing the pattern was one thing. Breaking it was something else entirely. But he was tired. Tired of guessing. Tired of excuses. Tired of pouring warmth into voids and calling it love.

The right people wouldn't make him guess. Wouldn't require excuses. Wouldn't leave him perpetually uncertain of his worth in their eyes.

It was time to stop settling for anything less.
