They reached the edge of a village by midmorning—not planned, just where the path led. A small settlement tucked into a valley between hills, maybe twenty buildings clustered around a central square. Smoke rose from chimneys. The sound of a blacksmith's hammer rang out in steady rhythm.
Korrin stopped at the village boundary, where cultivated fields began replacing wild meadow. "I'm going to resupply. Might take an hour or two. You're welcome to come or wait here."
Sefiathan looked at the village—its orderly paths, its people moving with purpose between buildings. Something in him recoiled. Too much. Too many potential conversations, too many eyes, too many chances to say the wrong thing or be judged or—
"I'll wait here," he said.
Korrin nodded without judgment and headed toward the village center. Sefiathan found a spot at the forest edge where he could see the village but remain separate from it. Sat with his back against a tree, supposedly resting.
But his mind immediately began its cataloging. What was wrong with him that he couldn't just walk into a village? Why was he so uncomfortable around people? Normal people could resupply without anxiety. Could chat with merchants and blacksmiths and village folk without overthinking every word. His sister would have marched right in, efficient and capable. His father would have found the craftsmen, talked shop, made connections.
And here was Sefiathan, hiding at the tree line like a frightened animal.
The spiral gained momentum. He was almost thirty and still couldn't handle basic social situations. Still choosing solitude over connection. Still running away from—
He stopped himself. Noticed what he was doing.
Yesterday's lesson surfaced: feel it completely, watch it move, notice what else exists alongside it.
Okay. So he felt uncomfortable around villages. Around people. Around the possibility of being seen and found wanting. That was true.
What else was true?
He looked around from his spot at the forest edge. Noticed details he'd been too busy judging himself to see. A group of children playing some kind of game in the square, their laughter carrying across the distance. An older woman hanging laundry, methodical and unhurried. Two men having an animated conversation near the blacksmith's shop, hands gesturing as they talked.
Everyone absorbed in their own lives. No one looking toward the forest edge. No one aware that Sefiathan existed at all.
His fear of their judgment was entirely in his head. They weren't thinking about him. Weren't building cases against him. Weren't comparing him to some standard of acceptable behavior.
They were just—living. Doing their village things. Existing in their own constellations of feeling and thought.
And he was here, at the edge, existing in his.
The judgment he feared wasn't coming from them. It was coming from inside himself, projected outward.
Sefiathan sat with that for a while. Watched the village continue its rhythm without him. And slowly, something shifted. Not into confidence or sudden social ease, but into—clarity. He wasn't comfortable in villages. That was fine. It didn't mean something was broken in him. It just meant he preferred forest edges to town squares.
Some people threw themselves into social situations easily, naturally. Made friends with everyone, chatted with strangers, felt energized by crowds. That wasn't him. Maybe would never be him.
And maybe that was okay.
After about an hour, Sefiathan stood. Not because he'd suddenly become brave, but because staying at the forest edge felt like hiding from a judgment that wasn't actually there. He walked into the village—not confidently, not with his shoulders back and head high like some hero in a story. Just walked. One foot in front of the other.
Nobody stared. Nobody pointed. The children kept playing their game. The woman kept hanging laundry. The two men kept their animated conversation.
Sefiathan made his way to the well at the center of the square and refilled his waterskin. A woman drawing water nearby nodded politely to him. He nodded back. That was it. No interrogation, no judgment, no requirement that he be anything other than a traveler getting water.
He found Korrin at the general store, negotiating prices with a merchant who seemed more interested in gossip than profit. They were deep in conversation about someone named Halleck who'd apparently left his wife for a traveling performer, which had caused considerable village drama.
"This is Sefiathan," Korrin said when they noticed him. "We're heading to the Frostmere."
The merchant looked him over briefly. "Long walk. You'll want extra wool socks. Got some good ones from the northern traders last month."
That was it. No questions about why he was going, who he was, what he was running from. Just—practical advice about socks.
Sefiathan bought the socks. Also some dried fruit and a small jar of honey, remembering the honey-nut bars Korrin had shared. The merchant wrapped everything efficiently, still chattering to Korrin about Halleck's wife and whether she'd take him back.
They left the village together, supplies replenished, and returned to the path heading north. After they'd walked far enough that the village sounds faded behind them, Korrin spoke.
"You came in."
"Yeah."
"That was hard for you."
"Yeah." Sefiathan adjusted his pack, feeling the weight of new supplies. "I was sitting at the forest edge building a whole case about what was wrong with me. Why I couldn't just walk into a village like a normal person."
"And?"
"And then I realized the judgment I was afraid of wasn't actually there. Was just—me, judging myself. Projecting it onto people who weren't thinking about me at all."
Korrin was quiet for a moment. "My cousin used to do that. Convinced everyone was judging them when really—people were just doing their own thing. Too busy with their own lives to care much about anyone else's."
"Is that why you two fought?"
"Part of it. They kept asking me to reassure them that people didn't think badly of them. And I got frustrated because—I don't know. I wanted them to just throw themselves at people. Make the move. Stop waiting for permission or perfect circumstances."
Sefiathan thought about the reading's language—throw yourself at someone. Make a move. Stop plotting scenarios that never materialize.
"But maybe that's not how everyone works," he said slowly. "Maybe some people need to sit at the forest edge first. Work through their fear. Find their own way in rather than being pushed."
"Yeah." Korrin's voice was soft. "I think I missed that. Thought the only way to deal with fear was to bulldoze through it. Be bold. Force yourself into situations until they stopped being scary."
"Does that work?"
"For some people, maybe. Didn't work for my cousin. Just made them feel like their fear was a failing. Like they should be braver than they were." Korrin paused. "I should have just—let them take their time. Let them come in from the forest edge when they were ready."
They walked in silence for a while, the path climbing gradually through scattered trees. Sefiathan thought about throwing yourself at someone—not necessarily romantic someone, but life-someone. The world. Experience. The possibility of connection.
He'd been waiting for perfect circumstances. For the fear to disappear. For confidence to arrive and make everything easy. But maybe that wasn't how it worked. Maybe you just—walked in from the forest edge when you could. Made small moves. Filled your waterskin. Bought socks. Said hello to a stranger and let that be enough.
Not bulldozing through fear, but not letting it keep you frozen either.
Around midday they stopped to rest near a stream. Korrin unpacked the supplies they'd bought—fresh bread from the village baker, cheese, apples. They ate together, the food tasting better for being fresh instead of preserved.
"I spent my whole life waiting for someone to invite me in," Sefiathan said. "Waiting for permission to exist in spaces. To take up room. To be seen." He bit into an apple, the crisp sweetness sharp on his tongue. "But I think maybe—nobody's going to invite me. I just have to walk in. Even if it's uncomfortable. Even if I'm not sure I belong."
"That sounds brave."
"It doesn't feel brave. Feels like—I don't know. Resignation? Acceptance? Just realizing that the invitation I was waiting for isn't coming."
"Maybe that's what brave is." Korrin lay back in the grass, closing their eyes against the sun. "Not the absence of fear. Just deciding the limbo of waiting is worse than the risk of moving."
Sefiathan thought about that. The limbo he'd been in for years—standing at forest edges, waiting to be invited, building cases against himself for not being braver. And how walking into the village, uncomfortable as it was, felt better than staying frozen in fear of judgment that wasn't even there.
That afternoon, as they walked through increasingly hilly country, Sefiathan practiced small moves. When they passed another traveler on the path, he said hello first instead of waiting for them to initiate. When they stopped to gather water from a spring, he asked Korrin about their herd instead of waiting for Korrin to volunteer information. Small things. Tiny moves forward.
Not transformative. Not suddenly confident or socially graceful. Just—slightly less frozen than before.
By evening they'd climbed high enough that the air had real bite to it. They made camp in a sheltered spot between boulders, the wind cutting above them but calm where they settled. The Thornspire Mountains were closer now, their peaks sharp against the sky.
"How many more days?" Sefiathan asked as they built the fire.
"To the Frostmere border? Five, maybe six depending on weather." Korrin arranged wood with practiced efficiency. "To the gathering, another two days beyond that."
A week. Maybe more. A week of walking with someone who didn't require him to be anything other than what he was. Who let him sit at forest edges when he needed to, but also noticed when he walked into villages.
"Thank you," Sefiathan said. "For letting me come with you."
"You're good company." Korrin looked up from the fire. "Quiet, but in a comfortable way. And you don't make me feel weird about my own stuff."
"Your cousin stuff?"
"Yeah. And other things." They sat back, watching flames catch and grow. "It's easy to be around someone who's also figuring it out. Don't have to pretend I have answers."
The fire settled into steady burning. Stars began appearing as darkness fell. And Sefiathan thought about moves—small ones, uncomfortable ones, the kind that didn't feel dramatic but changed things anyway.
Walking into villages. Asking questions. Saying hello first. Buying socks and filling waterskins and existing in spaces even when you weren't sure you belonged.
His melody hummed in his chest, and tonight it had a quality of motion. Not confident motion—more like tentative steps forward. Testing weight on new ground. Moving from the forest edge toward the center, slowly, uncomfortably, but moving.
Tomorrow he'd keep practicing. Small moves. Tiny risks. Throwing himself at life in the most gentle way possible—not bulldozing, just—stepping forward when he could.
Even when it was uncomfortable.
Especially when it was uncomfortable.
Because the limbo of waiting hurt more than the risk of rejection.
