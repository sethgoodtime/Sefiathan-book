# **Day 174 — June 24, 2026**

The morning came with wind that carried sand in stinging ribbons across the courtyard. Sefiathan woke before dawn, curled against the base of one of the warming pillars he'd spent yesterday tending. The stone still held faint heat against his back—not much but enough to notice. Enough to remind him he'd made a difference even if the difference was subtle.

The scholars were already moving when he stood and stretched stiff muscles. Thessaly had organized teams to continue uncovering the intentional burial patterns while Maren led a group documenting the warming effect Sefiathan's attention had created in the central structures. Everyone had clear tasks and purposes except him. He was supposed to continue the warming work but something about repeating yesterday's process felt wrong. Like he'd be performing understanding instead of genuinely discovering.

Korrin found him standing uncertain in the half-light. "You look lost."

"I don't know what to do today. Yesterday worked because I didn't know what would happen. Today I'd just be doing it again because it worked before."

"So do something different."

"Like what?"

Korrin shrugged, tail flicking away a sand-fly. "Whatever feels true instead of what feels safe."

The words settled uncomfortably because Sefiathan knew exactly what felt true and it terrified him. He wanted to go deeper into the ruins. Alone. Without witnesses or documentation or scholarly oversight. Wanted to sit with the most buried, most forgotten parts and see what his attention found there. But that meant walking away from the group's applause. Walking away from yesterday's success. Walking away from being useful in ways people could see and measure and validate.

The mirrors that reflected his image back had become a kind of prison. Everyone was pleased with what he'd done yesterday. Impressed by the warming stones. Excited about his unique abilities. And he could keep giving them that—could keep being the resonance mage who woke forgotten things in publicly documented ways. Could keep performing the role they wanted instead of discovering what his magic actually needed to do.

Breaking those mirrors meant risking their disappointment. Meant possibly finding nothing in the deeper ruins. Meant being alone with just the raw truth of who he was without witnesses to validate the experience. No audience. No applause. Just him and dormant stone and whatever happened when he stopped trying to be impressive.

Calantha emerged from her tent as he stood paralyzed by the choice. She took one look at his face and understood immediately. "You want to go somewhere alone."

"Yeah."

"And you're afraid people will think you're abandoning the work."

"I'm afraid I'll go off alone and find nothing and waste time everyone's counting on."

"That's not really what you're afraid of."

Sefiathan met her eyes and knew she was right. He wasn't afraid of wasting time. He was afraid of losing their approval. Afraid that if he stopped doing what had worked yesterday, people would realize he didn't actually know what he was doing. Would see him as unreliable, unpredictable, someone who couldn't be counted on to reproduce results. The validation had felt good—being useful, being valued, being the odd one whose strangeness finally mattered. Walking away from that toward uncertain solitude felt like rejecting the first real acceptance he'd found.

"The mirrors are a prison," Calantha said quietly. "Being what people expect because it makes them comfortable. Performing the version of yourself that gets applause. It feels safe but it's actually just another cage."

She was right and Sefiathan hated how right she was. He'd been so focused on claiming authority over his domain that he'd accidentally created new expectations to live up to. New ways to perform. New mirrors reflecting back an image of competent resonance mage instead of just odd Sefiathan who noticed things sideways.

Thessaly called the morning meeting and Sefiathan made himself speak before fear could silence him. "I want to explore the deeper ruins today. The parts that are most buried. Alone."

Several scholars looked confused. Maren frowned slightly. "We were hoping you'd continue with the central courtyard. The warming effect is measurable there. We can document—"

"I know. But I think there's something deeper that needs attention more than documentation."

Thessaly studied him with that assessing look she'd used before. "This is you following your instincts about your own magic."

"Yes."

"Will you be able to report what you find?"

"I don't know. Maybe. If there's anything to report."

The silence stretched uncomfortable and Sefiathan felt his resolve wavering. This was exactly what he'd been afraid of—their disappointment, their doubt, their realization that he couldn't be relied on to do predictable useful things. But he didn't apologize. Didn't soften the request. Didn't offer to compromise by bringing someone to document. Just stood there with his truth and let them decide what to do with it.

"All right," Thessaly said finally. "You know your magic better than we do. Follow what feels necessary."

The permission felt less like validation and more like being released into uncertainty. No audience. No witnesses to prove he'd done something worthwhile. Just him and his odd attention and whatever the deepest ruins held. It should have felt like freedom but mostly it felt terrifying.

He gathered supplies—water, dried fruit, a small lantern even though he didn't expect to need it. Korrin walked with him to the edge of the excavated area where sand-covered structures disappeared into dunes that might hide anything or nothing.

"You're doing the brave thing," Korrin said.

"Doesn't feel brave. Feels like I might be making a mistake."

"Maybe. But you're making it honestly instead of safely. That counts for something."

Sefiathan walked into the dunes alone and the world narrowed to just sand and stone and silence. The ruins here were barely visible—suggestions of walls beneath accumulated centuries. He could have stayed in the courtyard where his presence made measurable differences. Could have kept warming stones people could document. Could have been useful in ways that earned continued approval. But that would have been choosing the mirror over the truth. Choosing applause over authenticity. Choosing to be what people wanted instead of discovering what he actually was.

The first deeply buried structure he found was just fragments—broken pillars that didn't even rise above the sand. He sat beside them anyway, letting his attention settle without expectation. The warmth didn't come. The resonance didn't build. Just dead stone that had been dead so long it barely remembered being anything else.

He stayed anyway. Not because it was working but because this was the truth. Sometimes his magic found nothing. Sometimes his attention landed on things too dead to wake. Sometimes being odd and intense and notice-everything didn't matter at all. And he needed to sit with that without witnesses. Needed to know he could be ineffective and still be himself. Still be worth his own attention even when he wasn't useful to anyone else.

The sun climbed higher and Sefiathan moved deeper into the dunes. Found more fragments. More suggestions of structures. More stone that responded to his presence with nothing but indifference. The loneliness was profound but it was honest. No one watching meant no one to disappoint. No one documenting meant no pressure to produce results. Just him sitting with dead things in the desert heat, being exactly as strange and useless as he actually was.

Around midday he found something different. Not more impressive—actually less visible than the fragments he'd passed. Just a single carved stone buried so deeply he almost missed it entirely. But when he brushed sand away from the surface, the carving showed a pattern he recognized. The same intentional spiral he'd seen in his mother's garden stones. The same design that marked places where attention had been focused with love.

He sat with the stone and let his notice-everything tendency settle completely. Not trying to wake it. Not trying to prove anything. Just being present with this fragment someone had carved with care and then buried with intention. Preserved against time. Protected through forgetting. Waiting for someone odd enough to sit with it without needing it to be impressive.

The warmth came slowly, building from the spiral outward. Not waking the stone but warming it toward memory. Toward recognition that it had mattered once. That it still mattered now. That being buried and broken and forgotten didn't erase the love that had made it.

Sefiathan sat with the warming stone until the shadows lengthened and no one witnessed the tears on his face. This was the raw truth of who he was without validation. Someone who cared about forgotten things. Someone whose attention warmed toward tenderness instead of achievement. Someone strange enough to find meaning in buried spirals no one else would bother uncovering.

The loneliness was real but it wasn't prison. It was just what solitude felt like when you stopped performing for mirrors. When you broke free from familiar applause and walked into territory that offered no audience. When you chose authentic inefficiency over validated productivity.

He stayed until sunset painted the dunes amber and rust before finally turning back toward camp. The carved stone stayed warm under his hand—not dramatically, not measurably, just genuinely. Just real.

No witnesses. No validation. Just truth.
