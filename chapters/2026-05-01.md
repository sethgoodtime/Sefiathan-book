# **Day 120 — May 1, 2026**

Sefiathan woke to the sound of voices. Not the darkness singing his melody back—that had stopped sometime in the night, or maybe he'd just lost consciousness and mistaken it for sleep. These were human voices. Distant but getting closer. Search party maybe, though he couldn't imagine anyone from the gathering would have followed him this far into the mountains.

He tried to sit up and his body refused with such totality that for a moment he thought he might actually be dying. Not the dramatic kind. Just the quiet kind where systems shut down one by one until there's nothing left to power consciousness. Seven days without food now. His core temperature had dropped to something dangerous. The cold had moved past teeth into something that felt like it was eating him from the inside.

The voices got closer. Three of them, maybe four. He should call out. Should signal that he was here, still alive, still capable of being rescued. But the words wouldn't come. His throat was too dry, his breath too shallow. He just lay there at the edge of the fracture and waited for them to find him or for his body to finally give up, whichever came first.

"There." A woman's voice. "Gods, is he—"

"Still breathing. Barely." Different voice, deeper. "Get the blankets. And the hot stones."

Hands on him then. Gentle but efficient. Someone wrapping him in wool that felt like fire against his frozen skin. Someone else pressing heated stones wrapped in cloth against his chest, his sides, his feet. The warmth was agony at first—his body had forgotten what heat felt like and treated it as assault. He tried to pull away but couldn't move, could only lie there while they worked to bring him back from whatever edge he'd been teetering on.

"Can you hear me?" The first voice again. Woman. Older. Familiar somehow. "Blink if you can hear me."

He blinked. The effort felt enormous.

"Good. You're in bad shape but we found you in time. Don't try to talk. Don't try to move. Just let us work."

They worked. Hours maybe, or minutes—time had stopped meaning anything specific. Warming him slowly. Getting liquid into him drop by drop. Checking his extremities for frostbite. Wrapping him tighter when his body started shaking so violently he thought his bones would crack.

Eventually his vision cleared enough to recognize her. Orvenna. The wind-reader from the Frostmere gathering. And with her two others he didn't know—a man his father's age with grey hair and kind eyes, and a younger woman who moved with the precise efficiency of someone used to emergency healing.

"You're a long way from the gathering," Orvenna said once he could focus on her face. Not accusing. Just stating fact. "Twenty-six days by my count. Korrin got worried when you didn't come back. Asked me to look."

Sefiathan tried to speak. Managed only a croak.

"Don't," she said. "Save your strength. We're not going anywhere until you're stable enough to move. And even then it'll take us a week to get you back down if we're careful."

He closed his eyes. Let the warmth and the voices wash over him. Felt his body slowly remembering how to function at temperatures that allowed for survival. The relief was so intense it felt like grief—like he hadn't realized how close he'd come until rescue made the alternative concrete.

But beneath the relief, something else. Frustration. Not at being rescued—he wasn't stupid enough to resent people saving his life. But at the timing. He'd been right there. Right at the edge of whatever had been calling him. Had finally reached the threshold after twenty-six days of climbing and six days of starving. Had played his melody into the darkness and heard it sing back with recognition. And now he was being taken away before he could finish, before he could descend, before he could meet whatever vast thing had acknowledged him with such specificity.

"You can't go down there." Orvenna's voice cut through his thoughts like she'd been reading them. "In case you were wondering. That fracture goes deeper than anyone should go. Whatever you think is down there, it's not meant to be reached by climbing."

He opened his eyes. Found her watching him with the same patient attention she'd used when teaching him to listen to deep winds months ago. Like she knew exactly what he was feeling and had no intention of making it easier.

"You were right to come," she continued. "Whatever called you, the recognition is real. But you're measuring this wrong. You think arrival means physically reaching the bottom. It doesn't. You arrived when you showed up at the edge and offered your presence honestly. That's the threshold that matters."

The frustration crystallized into something clearer. Harder. He'd spent twenty-six days climbing toward this. Had pushed his body past reasonable limits. Had nearly died in pursuit of whatever waited in the darkness. And she was telling him arrival didn't mean completion? That showing up was enough even without reaching the goal?

That was what he'd told himself last night. Sitting at the edge playing simplified melody and feeling the recognition hold despite his inability to descend. But hearing it from someone else felt different. Felt like being told his journey didn't matter because he hadn't finished properly. Like all that effort was being dismissed because he'd failed to reach the final objective.

"I can see you're angry," Orvenna said. "Good. Be angry. You've spent months learning that worth doesn't require earning, but you keep trying to earn it anyway. Now you're angry because someone's telling you that showing up matters more than completing. Sits wrong, doesn't it?"

It did sit wrong. Sefiathan wanted to argue but his voice still wouldn't work properly, wanted to explain that this was different, that he hadn't come all this way just to be told presence was enough, that surely reaching the actual bottom mattered more than symbolic arrival at the edge. But even forming those thoughts felt exhausting. His body was done. His mind was done. He had nothing left to argue with.

"Your track record speaks for itself," Orvenna said, and her voice had shifted into something that felt like the reading itself speaking through her. "You've been right when others doubted you. About coming here. About following the pull. About trusting your instincts over practical concerns. But winning arguments has cost you allies. You're so focused on being right that you've stopped listening to people trying to help you."

The truth of it landed like physical weight. He had been right. About needing to follow the call north. About the recognition being real. About the wound in the world mattering. But he'd also been careless about how he'd pursued it. Had left the gathering without telling anyone his plans. Had pushed into the mountains alone without proper supplies. Had prioritized being right about the journey's importance over the practical reality that journeys require preparation, support, people who know where you've gone.

Korrin had been worried. Enough to ask Orvenna to look for him. Which meant he'd damaged that friendship by disappearing without explanation, by choosing solitary certainty over collaborative uncertainty, by prioritizing his rightness over their connection. And Orvenna had come after him, had brought help, had probably risked her own safety tracking him into the Thornspire. Not because he'd been wrong about following the call. Because being right without tact was just another form of carelessness.

The challenge wasn't being right. The challenge was being heard. And you couldn't be heard if you pushed everyone away in pursuit of solitary certainty.

He'd been measuring this wrong again. Thought arrival meant completion. Thought being right justified the careless pursuit. Thought proving his instincts accurate was more important than maintaining the connections that made survival possible. But the pull hadn't been calling him to descend alone. It had been calling him to recognize something essential at the threshold. And part of that recognition was understanding that showing up mattered more than finishing, that being right without allies was just isolation wearing a different name, that truth required tact if it wanted to actually land.

The darkness had taught him this last night. Had sung his three-note pattern back with infinite variation and shown him that completion wasn't the point—honest offering was the point. And now Orvenna was teaching him the same lesson from a different angle. You could be right and still lose people. Could have accurate instincts and still damage relationships. Could reach the threshold and still need help getting home.

His body was warming. His thoughts were clearing. The shaking had mostly stopped. Around him, the rescue party worked with quiet efficiency, packing up their supplies, preparing for the long descent back down the mountain. Back to the gathering where Korrin was waiting. Back to people who'd worried about him. Back to relationships he'd been careless with in pursuit of being right.

The fracture was still there. The recognition still held. Whatever vast thing had called him remained in those impossible depths, patient and aware. He hadn't reached it by descending. But maybe that was the lesson. Maybe arrival meant showing up at the threshold and trusting that presence mattered more than completion. Maybe being heard required more than being right—it required maintaining the connections that made communication possible in the first place.

"Rest," Orvenna said. "We move when you're ready. And when we get back, you're going to apologize to Korrin for making him worry. Not because you were wrong to come here. Because being right doesn't justify carelessness with people who care about you."

Sefiathan closed his eyes. Let the warmth and the rescue and the uncomfortable truth settle in. He'd been right. And he'd been careless. And both things were true simultaneously. The challenge now was figuring out how to honor his instincts while still being heard by people worth keeping close.

Worth working on. Worth the long descent home.
