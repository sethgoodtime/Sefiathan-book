# **Day 159 — June 9, 2026**

Sefiathan woke at dawn with the uncomfortable realization that he'd been treating good things like they were evidence of impending disaster. Not obviously—he wasn't walking around expecting catastrophe. But there was this subtle bracing he did whenever something pleasant happened. Someone smiled at him genuinely and he immediately started cataloging what he might have done to earn it, what he might need to do to keep it. Calantha invited him to sit by her fire and he spent the whole time half-waiting for her to realize he wasn't that interesting. Even his work with Orvenna—every time she said something affirming, some part of him tensed, waiting for the other shoe to drop.

He'd built walls against disappointment so high they'd become a prison. That was what yesterday's conversation with Calantha had been circling—this way they both had of treating pleasure like a trap. Like good things only happened as setup for inevitable letdowns. Like accepting that life could be nice was naive or dangerous or setting yourself up to be hurt worse later.

The Co-Star theme sat uncomfortably: accept that you deserve a nice life. Stop treating pleasure like a mistake waiting to happen. It felt impossibly vulnerable. Almost offensive in its simplicity. Like someone telling you to just relax when you've spent years developing excellent reasons not to.

He found Orvenna before breakfast, sitting in her usual spot where she could feel deep wind currents through the ground. She didn't acknowledge him immediately but shifted her weight slightly—the small tell that meant he could speak.

"How do you know when to trust good things?" The question came out more raw than he'd intended.

"You don't." Orvenna opened her eyes. "You just stop treating them like evidence of future betrayal."

"But what if they are?"

"What if they're not?" She stood, brushed frost from her trousers with weathered hands. "You've been here almost five months. Have I betrayed your trust?"

"No."

"Has Korrin?"

"No."

"Calantha?"

"No, but—"

"But you're still waiting for it." Not cruel, just observing. "Still bracing for the moment when all this good becomes bad. Still treating every kind look like it's hiding a trap. Still building walls against disappointment that have become a prison."

The accuracy stung. Sefiathan had thought he'd been working on this—learning to accept love without earning it, trusting genuine connection, letting himself be authentically odd. But apparently he'd been doing all that while still fundamentally not believing good things could last. Still treating pleasure like a mistake that would eventually correct itself. Still waiting for life to remember he didn't deserve nice things and adjust accordingly.

"How do you break that?" he asked.

"Stop protecting yourself from disappointment you're inventing." Orvenna started walking toward the gathering center and Sefiathan fell into step beside her. "Life will hurt sometimes. That's guaranteed. But you're hurting yourself constantly by refusing to believe good things when they happen. Spending all your energy bracing against disasters that might never come. That's not protection. That's just choosing to suffer preemptively."

They reached the fire circle where others were starting to gather for breakfast. Korrin waved from across the space and Sefiathan felt that familiar clench—the automatic bracing against the moment when Korrin would realize he didn't actually want to be friends with someone this odd. Never mind that they'd been traveling together for months now. Never mind that Korrin had never given any indication of finding Sefiathan burdensome. The walls were so automatic he barely noticed them anymore. Just constant low-level defense against good things turning bad.

Calantha joined them midway through breakfast, settling beside Sefiathan with the easy comfort of actual friendship. "I talked to them last night."

"The person you're in love with?"

"Yeah. Told them I'd been performing instead of just existing. That I wanted to try being actually myself even though it was terrifying." She picked at her food, not quite looking at him. "They said they'd been wondering when I'd stop trying so hard to impress them. Said they liked me better when I was just being weird."

"That's good."

"Yeah. It is." She finally looked up, something vulnerable in her expression. "But I keep waiting for them to change their mind. Keep expecting them to realize I'm not actually that impressive when I'm not performing. Keep treating their affection like it's a temporary condition that will correct itself once they get to know me better."

The recognition was sharp and immediate. That was exactly what Sefiathan had been doing. Treating every good thing like it was on loan. Temporary. Something that would be recalled once whoever granted it realized their mistake. Never quite believing he deserved any of it, so never quite able to relax into it.

"The walls we build against disappointment become prisons," he said quietly.

"What?"

"Something Orvenna told me this morning. We're both doing it—treating good things like traps. Building walls to protect ourselves from future hurt. But the walls just keep us locked in constant bracing. Constant defense. Never actually able to experience pleasure because we're too busy preparing for when it ends."

Calantha was quiet for a moment. Then: "So how do we stop?"

"I don't know yet. But I think it starts with noticing we're doing it. Catching ourselves in the act of treating pleasure like it's suspicious. And then choosing to believe it anyway. Even though it feels dangerous."

After breakfast, Sefiathan went back to the dying tree. It had become a kind of practice space—somewhere he could sit with uncomfortable things without an audience, without performing understanding, without trying to control outcomes. The tree was still mostly sick but that patch of healthier bark had grown noticeably over the past days. Not because he was forcing healing but because he kept showing up, kept paying genuine attention, kept letting his odd resonance work without micromanaging it.

Today he just sat with the recognition of how much energy he'd been spending on walls. All the good things that had happened this year—finding his melody, learning to take up space, developing real friendships, discovering his resonance magic, getting actual teaching from Orvenna. He'd experienced all of it through a filter of not quite believing it. Not quite trusting it would last. Always keeping one eye on the exit, always maintaining emotional distance, always ready for the moment when it would all prove temporary.

That was exhausting. Not in a dramatic way but in a constant low-grade drain. Like running a program in the background that used up processing power without producing anything useful. All that energy spent on protection that wasn't actually protecting him from anything except the possibility of fully experiencing his own life.

The tree beside him was dying from root disease but that small patch remembered health. Remembered being fully alive without constantly bracing for death. Sefiathan touched the healthier bark with careful fingers. Felt the subtle difference—not just physical but something deeper. This part of the tree had stopped treating life like a temporary condition. Had stopped building walls against inevitable collapse. Had just let itself be alive while it could.

That look someone gave you, that unexpected bonus—they're not traps. The thought landed with uncomfortable clarity. Calantha's friend liking her better when she was weird. Korrin genuinely enjoying his company. Orvenna actually wanting to teach him. The melody he'd created that somehow mattered to people. The resonance magic that was real and valuable and his. None of it was a trap. None of it was secretly bad. It was just good things happening and his job was to accept them as good instead of constantly preparing for when they'd turn bad.

He spent the afternoon practicing. Korrin came to sit with him and instead of bracing for rejection, Sefiathan just let himself enjoy the company. Calantha brought him tea and instead of cataloging what he might owe her, he just said thank you and meant it. A young wind-reader asked him about his melody and instead of preparing for mockery, he sang a few lines and accepted her genuine interest.

Each small moment of accepting good without bracing felt terrifying. Like standing on a cliff edge. Like removing armor in the middle of battle. But underneath the fear was something else—a lightness he hadn't felt in years. Like setting down a weight he'd been carrying so long he'd stopped noticing how heavy it was.

The walls against disappointment had become a prison. The protection had become the problem. And maybe the work was just noticing that and choosing differently. Choosing to believe pleasure when it happened. Choosing to accept kind looks as actually kind. Choosing to trust unexpected bonuses weren't secretly traps but just good things being good.

The evening came soft and cool. The tree was still dying but more of it remembered health now. Sefiathan sat beside it without bracing, without walls, without constant preparation for when good things would end. Just existing in a life that could be nice if he let it.
