# **Day 173 — June 23, 2026**

The ruins were older than anyone had estimated. Sefiathan could feel it in the stones even before he touched them—this deep exhaustion in the architecture, like buildings so tired of standing they'd forgotten they had the option to fall. The scholars had given him space as promised, spreading out to document the visible structures while he sat alone with the central courtyard's broken pillars.

He'd been here since just after dawn, letting his attention settle into the stone without forcing anything. The silence was different from the Frostmere's wind-song or the Deepwood's living hush. This was absence. Not peaceful quiet but the hollow that remained when something vital had drained away centuries ago.

Korrin had checked on him once, mid-morning, bringing water and dried fruit. "Anything happening?"

"Not yet. The stones are so dead they don't even remember being alive."

"You need anything?"

"Just time."

Korrin had left him there and Sefiathan kept sitting. Kept paying attention without expectation. Kept being present with dormant stone that had nothing to offer him except its long waiting. This was the work he'd claimed authority over—not because he knew how to do it right but because his weird attention was the only thing that might work at all.

The thing he kept noticing was how much easier it would be to hide behind tasks. To document things in his journal, to sketch the architectural features, to make himself useful in conventional ways instead of just sitting here doing apparently nothing. His hands wanted occupation. His mind wanted to catalog observations. His whole self wanted to prove he was being productive, being valuable, being worth the expedition's investment in bringing him here.

But that would be exactly what the reading had warned against. Hiding behind efficiency when what was needed was warmth. Showing he was capable when what mattered was showing he cared. Using practical help as armor against the vulnerability of just being present with something that needed attention more than action.

The stones didn't need him to be useful. They needed him to notice them. To sit with their death without trying to immediately fix it. To let his odd attention warm them back toward remembering before pushing for results.

Around noon, something shifted. Not dramatic but real—the stone under his hand grew fractionally warmer. Not responding to his touch but responding to his presence. To the fact that he was still here, still paying attention, still willing to sit with dormant architecture without demanding it wake up on his schedule.

Sefiathan kept his breathing steady and didn't move. Let the warmth spread slowly through the pillar he was touching. The resonance wasn't building like it had with the practice stones Maren had given him. This was subtler. More like coaxing than waking. Like the difference between shouting someone awake and sitting beside them until they naturally stirred.

The warmth reached the next pillar. Then the one after that. Small progressions, each taking long minutes. The courtyard slowly remembering it had been a place where things happened. Where people gathered. Where intention and architecture had worked together for purposes now forgotten.

Thessaly appeared in the early afternoon, careful not to interrupt but close enough to observe. Sefiathan didn't acknowledge her directly but knew she was watching. Documenting. Taking notes on process she couldn't fully understand but was trusting him to navigate. That trust mattered. Was the foundation for him being able to work at all.

"The stones are warming," he said quietly, not breaking his attention from the pillars. "But they're so tired. They've been dead so long they don't really remember what being alive felt like."

"Can you wake them?"

"I don't know yet. Maybe they don't want to be woken. Maybe what they need is just someone sitting with them in their exhaustion instead of demanding they get up and be functional again."

Thessaly was quiet for a long moment. "That's not what I expected you to say."

"It's not what I expected either." He shifted slightly, finding a more comfortable position. "But I think there's a difference between waking something and warming it. Between making it active and just making it less alone."

She left after that and Sefiathan kept sitting. The warmth continued spreading but it wasn't building toward awakening. More like the stones were just thawing. Remembering what temperature other than cold desert stone felt like. Remembering they'd once been part of something that mattered to someone.

The work was tender in ways that made him uncomfortable. Not the clear authority he'd claimed yesterday—this definitive taking charge of his domain. This was softer. More vulnerable. Sitting with dying architecture and offering warmth without knowing if it would make any difference at all. Being present with need he couldn't fix through competence or capability.

Calantha found him as the afternoon heat began to ease. She didn't ask questions, just settled nearby with her own work. Something about her presence made it easier to stay with the stones. Like having witness to his tenderness. Like not having to hide that this was about caring rather than just being capable.

"Someone told me once," she said after a while, not looking at him, "that strength and tenderness aren't opposites. That you can be both at once without one canceling out the other."

Sefiathan understood what she was offering. Permission to be strong in his claiming of space yesterday while also being tender in his approach today. Permission to take charge of his domain while also working with softness instead of force. Permission to stop treating authority and gentleness like they couldn't coexist.

"I keep thinking I have to choose," he admitted. "Either be strong and take up space or be tender and accommodate others. Either be dominant about my magic or be considerate of people's needs. Like I can't be both at once."

"Maybe that's the fear talking. That if you're tender people will think you're weak. That if you show you care they'll dismiss your strength."

That landed too accurately. Sefiathan had spent yesterday claiming authority specifically because he was afraid of being seen as too soft, too accommodating, too willing to defer. Had been trying to prove he could be strong. Could be dominant. Could take charge without apology. But the overcorrection had its own trap—this idea that being powerful meant never being gentle. That taking up space required hardness instead of warmth.

The stones under his hands were warmer now, almost alive in places. But they weren't waking to functionality. They were just... less alone. Less forgotten. Less abandoned to their long death without anyone noticing they were here.

"I think maybe the work isn't about waking these ruins," he said slowly. "I think it's about sitting with them. About letting them know they still matter even though they're mostly dead. About offering warmth without demanding they become anything other than what they are."

Calantha smiled slightly. "That's very you."

"Is it?"

"Yeah. Your whole thing is noticing what gets overlooked. Paying attention to what everyone else dismisses. Being tender with things that don't seem to deserve tenderness. That's your strength. Not despite the gentleness but because of it."

Maren joined them as evening approached, bringing news that the other scholars had found what looked like intentional burial patterns in the sand. Like someone had covered the ruins carefully rather than just letting time bury them. Protected them. Preserved them for finding later.

"They were loved," Sefiathan said quietly. "These buildings. Someone loved them enough to make sure they'd survive being abandoned."

"How can you tell?"

"The warmth responds to that kind of attention. The stones remember being cared for. That's what's waking under my hands—not function but memory of mattering."

Maren studied the warming pillars with new understanding. "So your resonance doesn't just wake magic. It wakes... care? Attention? The memory of being important to someone?"

"I think so. I think that's what I do. What my weird attention is for. Reminding things they were loved even when they've forgotten. Even when they're too dead to be useful anymore."

The recognition settled in his chest with quiet certainty. This was his domain. Not waking active magic or restoring functionality. But warming forgotten things back toward remembering they mattered. Being tender with what everyone else had dismissed as beyond help. Offering attention to exhaustion without demanding it transform into productivity.

Thessaly called the group together at sunset to share findings. Sefiathan reported what he'd learned about the stones remembering care. About how the warmth wasn't building toward awakening but toward memory. About how maybe the ruins didn't need to be functional again—they just needed to be witnessed in their death with the same attention they'd been loved with in their life.

The scholars listened with genuine interest. Not dismissing his tender approach as insufficiently rigorous. Not demanding he push harder toward measurable results. Just trusting that his strange magic knew what it was doing even when it didn't look like conventional investigation.

"Tomorrow," Thessaly said, "we'll work on uncovering more of the intentional burial. Sefiathan, you'll continue warming the stones. We'll document both processes and see what emerges."

It was exactly the kind of leadership he'd been afraid to claim yesterday. Clear direction about his domain. Trust in his odd process. Space for tenderness alongside strength. Not having to choose between being capable and being caring.

The stars came out cold and bright and Sefiathan sat with the warm stones while the desert cooled around him. Someone had loved these ruins enough to bury them gently. To preserve them against time. To make sure they wouldn't be forgotten even in their dying.

And now someone was here again, offering attention to their exhaustion. Being tender with their long death. Showing that what they'd been still mattered even though they couldn't be what they'd been anymore.

Strong and tender. Both at once. No choice required.
