# **Day 126 — May 7, 2026**

The morning after reconciling with Korrin, Sefiathan woke to find himself thinking about his father's workshop. Not the recent version—cluttered and chaotic, tools misplaced, half-finished projects gathering dust—but the workshop from his childhood. The way it had been when his father still cared about the craft beyond its utility. When each piece was measured twice, when joints fit so precisely you couldn't see the seam, when the smell of fresh wood shavings meant something was being built with attention that bordered on reverence.

He hadn't thought about that version of his father's work in years. The memory felt dangerous somehow. Like acknowledging his father had once been different meant confronting what had changed, what had been lost, what story he'd been telling himself about why things fell apart.

But the memory persisted as he dressed, as he walked through the gathering toward the communal fire where breakfast was being prepared. His father teaching him to read wood grain. To feel for the direction that wanted to be followed rather than forced. To understand that working with material meant listening to what it was trying to become, not imposing your vision regardless of resistance.

That man had existed. Sefiathan could remember him clearly now that he'd stopped reflexively pushing the memory away. The version of his father who'd been patient, who'd explained rather than dismissed, who'd believed craftsmanship mattered beyond just producing functional objects. Before whatever had hardened him. Before Sefiathan had become a disappointment rather than a student.

The question that followed the memory: what story had he been telling about that loss?

Calantha found him standing near the fire, holding a bowl of porridge he'd forgotten to eat. "You look like you're somewhere else entirely."

"Thinking about my father," Sefiathan said. Then added, because honesty seemed important this morning: "The version of him I'd forgotten existed."

She sat beside him without invitation. They'd developed that kind of friendship over the months at the gathering—the kind where presence didn't require explanation. "Tell me."

So he did. Described his father's workshop as it had been. The precision. The attention. The belief that objects made with care carried something beyond mere function. And then described what it had become—the carelessness, the dismissiveness, the way his father had stopped caring about anything except getting work done quickly enough to pay bills.

"And you've been carrying that second version as the only truth," Calantha said when he'd finished. Not a question.

"I didn't realize there was another version to carry. Or I did, but I—" He stopped. Tried to find words for something he'd been doing without conscious awareness. "I put it away. The memory of him being different. Because if I acknowledged he'd once been the kind of father who taught patiently, then I'd have to ask what made him stop. What I did to make him stop."

"That's quite a story."

The words weren't cruel but they landed with weight. Sefiathan looked at her. "What do you mean?"

"The story where you're responsible for your father's loss of care. Where his hardness is your fault because you became a disappointment rather than a worthy student. That's the narrative you've been protecting by forgetting he was ever different." She paused. "Put down the album. The past isn't the problem. It's the story you keep telling about what was lost."

The Co-Star theme landed sideways through her words. Those pictures from years ago weren't windows to better times—they were traps. Not because the memory was false but because the story he'd built around it made the past into evidence of his inadequacy. His father had been capable of patience once, therefore his father's loss of patience was Sefiathan's failure. His father had cared about craft once, therefore his father's dismissal of Sefiathan's different magic meant Sefiathan wasn't worth careful teaching.

But that was a story. One version of what the memory could mean. Not fact. Not inevitable truth. Just one way his mind had organized the information to explain why things fell apart.

"What if he just changed?" Calantha continued. "People do that. Not because their children disappoint them but because life wears them down or priorities shift or the thing that once mattered stops mattering for reasons that have nothing to do with you. What if his hardness is his story, not yours?"

Sefiathan felt something crack open in his chest. The familiar shape of it—the belief that he'd failed to be worth his father's care, that his strangeness had cost him access to patient teaching, that if he'd just been different his father would have remained the man from the early memories. He'd been carrying that story for so long it felt like fact. Like the only possible explanation for what had been lost.

But it was just a story. One his mind had manufactured to make sense of pain. To give him something to fix, some way to earn back what he'd lost. If his inadequacy was the problem, then becoming adequate could solve it. The story gave him agency even if that agency was just the endless work of trying to prove his worth.

Except he'd spent a year learning that worth wasn't something you earned. That existing fully didn't require justification. That taking up space was a birthright, not a prize for adequate performance. And now here was the same lesson from a different angle: his father's changes weren't evidence of his failure. They were just changes. Human complexity playing out in ways that had their own logic, their own causes, their own narrative that existed completely separate from whether Sefiathan was disappointing.

"Today demands your attention, not yesterday," Calantha said. "The version of your father who taught you to read wood grain—he was real. But the story about why he stopped being that way? That's what hurts. Not the past itself but what you keep telling yourself the past means."

The porridge had gone cold in his hands. Sefiathan set it down carefully, watched steam rise from the communal fire, felt the weight of a story he'd been carrying without realizing it was optional. His father had changed. That was true. But the reasons didn't have to be about Sefiathan's inadequacy. Could be about his father's own losses, his own disappointments, his own slow hardening under pressures that had nothing to do with whether his son was worth patient teaching.

The chains were made of smoke. The story about being responsible for his father's loss of care—that was smoke. Looked solid. Felt real. But had no substance beyond what his mind kept manufacturing. He could notice the thought. Could watch it drift. Could choose not to feed it with continued belief.

Around them the gathering was beginning its day. Wind-readers were preparing for morning practice. Healers were tending to minor injuries. Someone was singing near the frozen marsh, their voice carrying clear and bright across the ice. Life happening now. In this moment. Demanding attention that wasn't caught in narratives about what yesterday meant.

Sefiathan thought about the fracture in the mountains. The darkness that had sung his melody back with recognition. The moment when he'd understood arrival didn't mean completion—that showing up at the threshold with honest presence was enough. That same lesson kept appearing from different angles. Stop measuring everything by some imagined destination. Stop telling stories about what past events mean about your worth. Stop feeding the smoke that looks like substance.

"Thank you," he said to Calantha.

She smiled. "For what?"

"For noticing I was somewhere else. For asking me to tell you. For pointing out I was trapped in a story rather than a memory."

"That's what friends do," she said simply. "We notice when you're in the album instead of the morning. We point out that today is happening and it's worth your attention."

Sefiathan looked around the gathering. Really looked. Not through the lens of what he was supposed to be doing or who he was supposed to be becoming. Just noticed what was actually here. Korrin talking with a group of younger centaurs, teaching them something with patient gestures. Orvenna watching him from across the fire, her expression unreadable but not unkind. The wind carrying familiar voices and unfamiliar songs. Spring asserting itself despite the northern cold. Life in motion. Present tense. Demanding attention that wasn't caught in stories about yesterday.

His father had been patient once. That was true. His father had hardened. That was also true. But the narrative that connected those facts through Sefiathan's inadequacy? That was optional. Just one story among many possible stories. And he could choose to put down the album. Could choose to stop rehearsing what was lost. Could choose to notice that today was happening and it was worth his full attention.

Worth working on. Worth the discomfort of releasing familiar narratives. Worth learning that the past wasn't the problem—it was the story he kept telling about what the past meant.

The smoke had no substance. He could step through it. Could choose presence over narrative. Could give today his attention instead of yesterday his grief.

The morning was cold and bright and happening now.
