# **Day 177 — June 27, 2026**

Sefiathan had been alone with the buried ruins for three more days. Each morning he walked deeper into the dunes while the scholars worked the excavated courtyard. Each evening he returned with sand in his hair and nothing useful to report. Just more carved stones. More intentional spirals. More fragments someone had loved enough to preserve but not enough to save from burial.

The loneliness had settled into something almost comfortable. Not the sharp isolation of being unwanted but the quiet truth of being exactly himself without anyone watching. He'd found seventeen spiral stones so far, scattered across the buried structures like breadcrumbs from someone who'd known these ruins would be forgotten. Who'd marked them anyway. Who'd cared enough to leave signs even though no one might ever read them.

His mother had taught him the spiral pattern when he was eight. Had shown him how to carve it into garden stones to mark places where attention had been focused with love. "It's not magic," she'd said. "It's just reminding yourself that you cared. That this spot mattered. That your attention was here and it was worth being here."

He'd carved dozens of spirals in Millhaven. In the garden. By the creek. On the fence post near his favorite sitting spot. Little marks of his odd attention scattered through his childhood like he was trying to prove he'd existed. Like he needed evidence that he'd been present in his own life. His father had called it vandalism once. Said he was defacing perfectly good wood with his strange compulsions.

But someone else had done the same thing here. Had marked these ruins with spirals before burying them. Had left proof of attention even as they abandoned the buildings to sand and time. And Sefiathan sat with each carved stone, warming them back toward memory, offering his own odd notice to marks of someone else's odd notice from centuries ago.

He wasn't measuring his worth by achievement anymore. That was the uncomfortable recognition that had settled in over these solitary days. He couldn't. There was nothing to achieve out here. No measurable progress. No validated results. Just him sitting with forgotten stones while the real work happened back at the courtyard where people could document and analyze and prove that effort mattered.

The flaws he obsessed over—his uselessness, his inefficiency, his tendency to care about things that didn't produce results—those weren't even visible out here. Nobody was watching him fail to be impressive. Nobody was counting the hours he spent with dead stone that refused to wake. Nobody was keeping a ledger of his inadequacy because he'd walked away from their attention entirely.

Put down the mental red pen. The reading's words had been circling his mind since yesterday. He'd been editing himself for so long it had become automatic. Every thought about his worth got marked up with corrections. Every moment of just being himself got footnoted with all the ways he was doing it wrong. The endless revisions of who he should be instead of who he actually was.

But the world needs you, not your endless revisions. That landed harder each time he sat with it. The buried ruins didn't need him to be anything other than someone willing to notice them. The carved spirals didn't need him to be impressive or productive or conventionally useful. They just needed his odd attention exactly as it was—strange and intense and willing to sit with forgotten things until they felt less alone.

His life was a precious gift. The phrase felt foreign in his mouth. He'd spent twenty-seven years treating his existence like something he had to earn. Like being alive was a privilege he needed to justify through achievement. Like his worth came from what he could produce rather than just the raw fact of being here, being himself, being odd in ways that didn't require correction.

The sun was brutal today, heat rising from sand in shimmering waves. Sefiathan had found another cluster of buried structures just after dawn—three walls forming a partial room, roof long gone, filled with sand that had drifted smooth and undisturbed. He'd been sitting here for hours, clearing sand from the walls one handful at a time, looking for more spirals. Not because it would produce anything. Not because anyone would validate the effort. Just because it felt true.

Two spirals on the north wall. One on the east. Each carved with the same careful attention his mother had taught him. Each marking where someone had focused love. Each saying I was here, I cared, this mattered to me even if it matters to no one else.

He pulled out his own carving knife—the one he'd used to make the wooden bird he'd left in the Deepwood months ago. Hadn't touched it since then. But looking at these ancient spirals, he felt the pull to add his own. To mark this buried room with proof of his attention. To say I was here too, I noticed, you're not forgotten.

The stone was softer than he expected, weathered by centuries of being buried. The spiral came easily under his blade. Not perfect—the curve wobbled in places, the line wasn't as clean as the ancient ones. But it was his. His mark. His proof that he'd been present in this moment, in this place, with these forgotten walls.

Nobody would see it. He wasn't going to report finding this room. Wasn't going to bring the scholars here to document and analyze. This could stay buried. This could be just for him. Just his odd attention meeting someone else's odd attention across the gap of centuries, leaving marks that said we cared about things that didn't matter to anyone else and that was enough.

The world needs you. Not the version of you that's been edited and revised. Not the person you think you should be. Not the careful performance of someone who's earned the right to exist. Just you. Raw and strange and obsessed with things nobody else would bother noticing.

Sefiathan sat back and looked at his carved spiral among the ancient ones. The flaws were obvious—the wobble, the imperfect depth, the way it didn't quite match the elegant certainty of the old marks. He could hear his father's voice calling it vandalism. Could hear his own internal editor marking all the ways it wasn't good enough. Could feel the familiar pull to apologize for taking up space on this wall, for adding his inadequate mark to something that had been perfect without him.

But he didn't. Didn't apologize. Didn't edit. Didn't revise the carving or his presence or his right to be here making marks that nobody would ever validate. Just let it be exactly what it was—proof that he'd existed in this moment. That his attention had been here. That his life, strange and inefficient as it was, had touched these stones with the same odd care someone else had offered centuries ago.

The mental red pen kept trying to correct him. Kept marking up his worth with all the ways he was doing life wrong. But he was practicing putting it down. Was practicing just being here without endless revisions. Was practicing treating his life like the precious gift the reading insisted it was instead of a rough draft that needed endless improvement before it could count as real.

Your life is a precious gift. Not your achievements. Not your usefulness. Not your carefully revised version of acceptable existence. Your life. The raw fact of you being here, being odd, being exactly who you are without apology.

Sefiathan touched his carved spiral one more time, then buried it again under careful handfuls of sand. Let it join the others in their long waiting. Let it be proof of attention that nobody needed to validate. Let it matter just because he'd decided it mattered, not because anyone else agreed.

The sun was setting when he finally stood, muscles stiff from sitting. He turned back toward the distant camp where people were doing real work, producing real results, being useful in ways that counted. And he didn't apologize for spending another day being strange in the buried ruins. Didn't revise his worth. Didn't measure himself by what he'd achieved.

Just walked back through the dunes carrying the truth that his life was a precious gift exactly as it was.
