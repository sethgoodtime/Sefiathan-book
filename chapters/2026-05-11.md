# **Day 130 — May 11, 2026**

The invitation came at breakfast, casual enough that Sefiathan almost missed its significance. Korrin sat down beside him with his own bowl of porridge and said, without preamble, "Tavis and I are heading to the hot springs this afternoon. You should come."

Should. Not could. Not an open-ended offer that left room for polite refusal. A statement that assumed his presence was wanted, that his company was desired rather than merely tolerated. The kind of invitation that didn't leave space for his usual spiraling about whether acceptance would make him appear dependent or refusal would seem ungrateful.

Sefiathan's first instinct was still to analyze. To catalog all the reasons Korrin might not actually want him there, might just be being polite, might feel obligated after their conversation about careless absence. But the thought arrived and he watched it drift. Noticed it was happening. Recognized the familiar pattern of turning every genuine offer into evidence of his inadequacy.

"Yeah," he said. "I'd like that."

Korrin nodded like it was the simplest thing in the world. "Good. We're leaving after lunch. Bring something warm—the walk back gets cold once the sun drops."

That was it. No negotiation. No careful navigation of unspoken expectations. Just an invitation offered and accepted, two friends making plans to do something together because that's what friends did. The ordinariness of it felt radical after years of treating every social interaction like a complex equation requiring precise calculation.

They left in early afternoon, the three of them walking together through the forest fringe where spring was beginning to assert itself more boldly. Buds on the birch trees had opened into small leaves. The frozen marsh was starting to show patches of mud where ice had melted. Birds Sefiathan couldn't name were singing territorial claims from the canopy. The world was waking up and he was moving through it with people who wanted his company.

Tavis talked most of the way, telling stories about his travels before coming to the gathering. He'd spent six months in the Wandering Dunes, learning to read weather patterns from a group of nomads who could predict sandstorms three days in advance. Before that he'd been studying with metallurgists in the Ashlands, trying to understand why certain alloys held enchantments better than others. His curiosity was voracious and undirected—he pursued whatever interested him without needing it to build toward some coherent career or purpose.

"Don't you worry about not having a focus?" Sefiathan asked. It came out more genuine than he'd intended, less masked by casual interest.

Tavis shrugged. "I did for a while. Felt like I should pick one thing and get really good at it instead of dabbling in everything. But then I realized I don't actually want to be an expert in anything specific. I want to know a little about everything. Want to be able to talk to the metallurgists and the wind-readers and the nomads. Want to collect understanding like some people collect objects." He grinned. "It's probably not the most practical approach."

"But it's what actually interests you," Korrin said.

"Yeah. And I'm done pretending I'm going to suddenly develop sustained focus on one topic when I've spent thirty years proving I won't."

The conversation felt significant in ways Sefiathan couldn't quite articulate. Tavis wasn't apologizing for being scattered. Wasn't treating his breadth of shallow knowledge as a failure to commit. He'd just accepted that this was how he engaged with the world and stopped fighting himself about it. Which was different from what Sefiathan had been doing—not because Sefiathan's approach was wrong but because he'd spent so much energy trying to justify it, to prove it was valid, to defend against accusations that mostly existed in his own mind.

What if you just did things because they interested you and stopped performing justification for imaginary critics?

The hot springs occupied a shallow basin about an hour's walk from the gathering. Steam rose from three pools fed by underground thermal vents, the water mineral-rich and smelling faintly sulfuric. The area around the pools was bare rock worn smooth by centuries of feet, surrounded by evergreens that seemed to thrive on the ambient warmth. Other people from the gathering had clearly been using the springs—there were towels hanging from low branches, carved wooden benches positioned near the largest pool, fire rings showing recent use.

But this afternoon they had the place to themselves. Korrin and Tavis stripped unselfconsciously and waded into the nearest pool. Sefiathan followed more slowly, aware of his body in ways that felt neither shameful nor performative. Just present. Just here. Just another person occupying space among people who weren't cataloging his flaws.

The water was almost too hot at first, his skin protesting before adjusting to the temperature. He found a smooth rock to sit on and let himself sink to his shoulders, feeling tension he hadn't realized he was carrying start to release from muscles that had been clenched against cold and uncertainty and the constant low-grade vigilance of trying to predict judgment.

They soaked in comfortable silence for a while. Korrin had his eyes closed, face tilted toward the sun filtering through the evergreen canopy. Tavis was watching water striders move across the surface where the hot spring met a cooler tributary. Sefiathan let his attention drift without trying to fix it on anything specific, just noticed sensation—heat, sulfur smell, bird calls, the slight movement of water as Korrin shifted position.

Being here felt different from other moments of connection he'd experienced over the year. Those had been significant because they were teaching him something, revealing some pattern he needed to recognize. But this wasn't teaching anything. This was just three people spending time together because they enjoyed each other's company. No crisis. No revelation. No profound recognition. Just the ordinary pleasure of being warm and comfortable among friends.

"I've been performing at peak capacity," Sefiathan said eventually. The words came out unprompted, following the thread of this morning's Co-Star theme without analyzing whether sharing was appropriate. "But mostly alone. Like I can do everything I'm supposed to be doing but it doesn't feel connected to anything outside myself."

Korrin opened his eyes but didn't look at him directly. "What would make it feel connected?"

"This, maybe. People running beside me instead of applauding from the sidelines." He paused, testing the metaphor. "Or not even running beside me. Just being present. Not evaluating whether I'm doing well but just existing in the same space while I do whatever I'm doing."

"You've been treating friendship like performance review," Tavis said. Not accusatory, just observational. "Like people are grading your social competence rather than just spending time with someone they like."

The recognition landed cleanly. That was exactly what he'd been doing. Treating every interaction as an evaluation. Every invitation as a test of whether he could navigate social expectations correctly. Every shared moment as evidence either for or against his worth as a friend. Which meant he'd been bringing vigilance to situations that just wanted presence. Had been performing where people just wanted him to exist.

"The spotlight feels emptier lately," Sefiathan admitted. "Because I've been standing in it alone. Proving I can handle things by myself. But that's—" He stopped. Found words for something that was still forming. "That's not actually what I want. I don't want to prove I can do everything alone. I want to do things with people. Want them to join my world instead of just watching from outside it."

"So let them," Korrin said simply. "Stop making it complicated. We're here. We want to be here. You don't have to earn that or justify it or prove you deserve it. Just accept it."

Sefiathan looked at his friends—Korrin stretched out in the hot water like he'd been born amphibious, Tavis now tracking the movement of clouds through the canopy, both of them completely comfortable being exactly who they were without performance or pretense. They'd joined his world not because he'd proven himself worthy but because they genuinely enjoyed his company. They were running beside him not because he'd asked perfectly or performed correctly but because that's what friends did.

The smoke had no substance. The belief that he had to do everything alone to prove his worth—that was just isolation wearing a protective name. The fear that needing people made him weak—that was just vigilance pretending to be strength. The conviction that he had to maintain peak performance alone or admit failure—that was just the spotlight creating shadows where there could be warmth.

"Make demands," Tavis said, reading from some internal list. "That's what the voice in my head keeps saying I'm not allowed to do. Ask for what I want. State preferences. Expect people to meet me halfway instead of just being grateful they tolerate my presence."

"What do you want?" Sefiathan asked.

"To stay here until the water prunes my fingers. To eat dinner with people I like. To not apologize for existing." Tavis grinned. "Pretty low bar honestly. But I've been setting the bar underground my whole life so low feels revolutionary."

They stayed in the hot springs until the sun began dropping toward the horizon, until Sefiathan's skin was pink and oversaturated with heat, until the ordinary pleasure of being warm among friends felt less like performance and more like baseline reality. Then they dressed and walked back through the forest in comfortable quiet, three people who'd spent an afternoon doing nothing significant except being present with each other.

No crisis resolved. No pattern revealed. No profound recognition of internal limitation.

Just friends. Just warmth. Just the simple revolutionary act of letting people join your world instead of performing alone in the spotlight.

The evening was cooling rapidly and the gathering was preparing dinner and Sefiathan was exactly where he wanted to be.
