**Day 112 — April 23, 2026**

The structure wasn't a structure at all. Sefiathan reached it on the twentieth day into the mountains and discovered it was a wound—a massive fracture in the ice that descended so deep the bottom disappeared into darkness that seemed to absorb light rather than simply lacking it. The edges were smooth, almost surgical, as if something had cut through the frozen valley floor with precision instead of violence.

He stood at the edge looking down and felt the pull in his chest focus to a single point somewhere in that impossible depth. This was what had been calling him. Not a place. An opening. A way through to whatever vast thing had been stirring beneath the Thornspire for months.

His supplies were gone. He'd eaten the last of his food two days ago, had been surviving on melted snow and the stubborn refusal to acknowledge how his body was starting to fail. Hunger had moved past discomfort into something sharper—a clarity that felt dangerous, like his mind was operating on emergency reserves and would crash soon if he didn't find sustenance. But he'd come too far to turn back now. Twenty days from the gathering. Two days from the last food. Standing at the edge of a wound in the world that led to something that had recognized him specifically.

He was wounded too. That was the thing he kept trying not to think about. Not physically—though his body was wearing thin, reserves depleting, strength fading. But the deeper wounds. The ones he carried from Millhaven. From his father. From twenty-seven years of measuring his worth by whether he justified the space he took up.

Those wounds existed. He could acknowledge that now. Could name them without treating them as evidence of failure. His father's emotional distance had hurt him. His years of feeling inadequate had left marks. The constant apologizing, the measuring, the calculating whether he was worth anyone's time—all of that had wounded something essential in him. The injuries were real. They'd shaped who he became. And pretending they didn't matter, pretending he should just move past them without processing, pretending his sensitivity made him weak—all of that had been another wound layered on top of the original ones.

But the reading that morning had said something that landed with uncomfortable precision: *You remember every hurt with perfect clarity. Stop treating these memories as evidence of your failure. They're just data points. The wounds exist. Acknowledge them, then use that sharp mind to plot your recovery.*

He'd been treating his wounds as proof of inadequacy. Evidence that he was too sensitive, too easily hurt, too incapable of handling what others managed with apparent ease. But that was wrong. The wounds weren't evidence of failure. They were just wounds. Real injuries from real experiences that had actually happened. Remembering them with clarity wasn't weakness. It was accurate accounting of what he'd survived.

The difference—the crucial difference he was only now beginning to understand—was between acknowledging wounds and letting them define him. Between recognizing he'd been hurt and treating those hurts as evidence that something was fundamentally wrong with him.

He sat down at the edge of the fracture, legs dangling into that impossible depth, and thought about all the ways he'd been wounded. His father's dismissiveness. The years of feeling like a burden. The romantic connections that fizzled because he cared too intensely too fast. The friendships that couldn't hold the weight of his particular kind of attention. Every time someone had pulled away, set a boundary, said "this is too much," he'd treated it as evidence of his inadequacy instead of recognizing that he'd simply been wounded by the collision between his depth and their limits.

The wounds were real. That was the revelation. Not imagined. Not exaggerated. Not evidence that he was too sensitive or needed to toughen up. Real injuries from real experiences that had actually left marks. And acknowledging that—naming the hurts clearly, remembering them with perfect accuracy, refusing to minimize or explain them away—that wasn't weakness. That was using his sharp mind to understand exactly what had happened so he could figure out how to heal.

His father had wounded him. Not through malice necessarily. Not because his father was evil or deliberately cruel. Just through repeated emotional distance, through years of treating Sefiathan's sensitivity as inconvenience, through subtle implications that the son he'd gotten wasn't quite the son he'd wanted. Those wounds existed independent of his father's intentions. They'd left marks whether his father acknowledged causing them or not.

And Sefiathan had spent years treating the memory of those wounds as evidence of his own failure. As proof that he was too sensitive, too needy, too incapable of handling normal family dynamics. Had turned the wounds inward and used them to justify all the ways he felt inadequate. Had let the injuries become the foundation for a whole structure of self-blame that treated being wounded as personal failing.

But wounds weren't failure. They were just damage from genuine harm. And he had a sharp mind—sharp enough to remember every hurt with perfect clarity, sharp enough to track patterns and recognize how injuries compounded over time, sharp enough to understand exactly how he'd gotten here. He could use that sharpness to plot recovery instead of using it to build more elaborate structures of self-blame.

The wind picked up, carrying ice crystals that stung exposed skin. Sefiathan pulled his coat tighter and kept sitting at the edge of the wound in the world. Somewhere far below, that ancient attention focused on him with patience that felt like recognition. Like it knew he needed this moment. This accounting. This acknowledgment of how he'd been injured before he could move toward healing.

He thought about the last twenty days alone in the mountains. Thought about how starvation had started stripping away all the careful layers of protection he'd built. How hunger made it harder to maintain the narratives he used to manage pain. How his body's depletion was forcing his mind into emergency clarity that couldn't afford elaborate self-deception anymore.

He was wounded. Multiple wounds layered over years. His father's distance. The feeling of being a burden. The romantic rejections. The friendships that couldn't hold his intensity. The constant measuring of his worth. All of those had left marks. Real marks. Data points of actual harm that he could track with the same precision he used to track everything else his sharp mind catalogued.

And acknowledging that didn't make him weak. It made him accurate. It meant he was finally seeing the injuries clearly instead of using them to build elaborate structures of self-blame. The wounds existed. They'd happened. They'd left marks. That was just truth. And truth could be worked with in ways that self-deception couldn't.

The question wasn't whether he'd been wounded. The question was what he did with that information now that he'd named it clearly. Did he keep treating the wounds as evidence of failure? Or did he use his sharp mind—the same mind that remembered every hurt with perfect clarity—to plot actual recovery instead of just building more sophisticated ways to blame himself for being injured?

He could feel the shape of healing starting to emerge. Not dramatic healing. Not sudden transformation. Just the careful, methodical work of using his analytical precision to understand exactly what needed attention. The wounds with his father required mourning the father he'd never had instead of trying to earn love from the father who existed. The romantic wounds required recognizing that his intensity wasn't too much—it just required people capable of meeting it. The friendship wounds required acknowledging that some people couldn't hold his particular kind of care and that limitation was about them, not him.

All of those required seeing the injuries clearly and then making deliberate choices about recovery. Required using his sharp mind for healing instead of self-destruction. Required treating the wounds as data points to be analyzed and addressed rather than evidence of fundamental inadequacy.

The sun was setting when he finally stood. His body protested—weak from hunger, depleted from twenty days of climbing, running on reserves that were nearly exhausted. But his mind was clearer than it had been in months. The wounds existed. He could name them. And now he could start plotting recovery with the same precision he'd used to track every hurt.

Tomorrow he'd descend into the wound in the world. Tomorrow he'd meet whatever had been calling him with such insistence. But tonight he made camp at the edge of the fracture and thought about healing. About acknowledging injuries without treating them as failure. About using his sharp, precise, damage-tracking mind to plot recovery instead of just cataloguing harm.

The wounds would heal. Not all at once. Not without deliberate work. But they would heal. Because he could finally see them clearly enough to know what needed attention.

That was enough to start.
