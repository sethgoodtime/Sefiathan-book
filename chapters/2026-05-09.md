# **Day 128 — May 9, 2026**

The problem started three days ago when Wendell asked if Sefiathan wanted company on the walk to check his snares. A simple question. A kind offer. Wendell was preparing to leave the gathering, heading back south with Maeve, and he'd been making small gestures like this all week—including Sefiathan in his routines, offering quiet companionship that didn't demand conversation. Normal friendship behavior.

Sefiathan had said yes. They'd walked together through the forest fringe, checked the snares Wendell had set for rabbits, talked idly about spring arriving faster this year than last. Easy. Comfortable. The kind of connection that should have felt uncomplicated.

But that night Sefiathan had lain awake cataloging the interaction. Analyzing whether he'd contributed enough to the conversation. Whether Wendell had actually wanted company or had just felt obligated to offer. Whether accepting the invitation made him appear weak, unable to occupy his own time. Whether declining would have been rude. The analysis spiraled into familiar territory—the exhausting work of trying to read minds, to predict judgment, to preemptively defend against accusations that existed only in his imagination.

By morning he'd decided to avoid Wendell. Not consciously. Just found himself taking different paths through the gathering. Eating at different times. Making himself unavailable when he saw Wendell approaching. Because if he wasn't around, he couldn't risk appearing dependent. Couldn't give anyone reason to think he needed help he couldn't handle alone.

Except now it was three days later and Wendell was leaving tomorrow and Sefiathan had successfully pushed away someone who'd been reaching out with genuine care. Had built walls to protect against accusations that hadn't been made. Had chosen isolation over the risk of appearing weak.

The realization hit during morning practice with Orvenna. She was teaching him to feel for deep currents—the underground winds that moved slow and strong beneath faster surface patterns. Sefiathan was supposed to be listening with his whole attention, but his mind kept returning to Wendell. To the three days of avoidance. To the expression on Wendell's face yesterday when Sefiathan had made an excuse about needing to help with something that clearly didn't need his help.

Orvenna stopped mid-instruction. "You're not here."

"Sorry. I'm—"

"Not sorry. Here or not here. Choose."

Sefiathan tried to pull his attention back but couldn't seem to anchor it. The self-judgment was too loud. He was failing at practice because he was too busy failing at friendship because he was too busy protecting against appearing weak. The spiral was familiar and exhausting and he wanted to be done with it but didn't know how to stop feeding the pattern.

"Take a break," Orvenna said. Not unkindly but not gently either. "You're doing that thing where you chew yourself instead of dealing with what's actually bothering you."

The directness was a relief. Sefiathan sat heavily on the frozen ground. "I pushed someone away."

"Why?"

"Because accepting his help felt like admitting I can't handle things alone."

Orvenna considered this. "And can you?"

"Can I what?"

"Handle things alone."

The question should have been simple but it landed complicated. Sefiathan thought about the month he'd spent climbing toward the fracture. The six days without food. The hypothermia. The moment when Korrin and Wendell and Maeve had found him barely conscious and pulled him back to survival. He couldn't have handled that alone. Would have died alone if they hadn't come looking.

But admitting that felt dangerous. Like opening a door to endless need. If he acknowledged he sometimes needed help, where did it stop? How much help was acceptable before it became dependency? How did you receive care without becoming a burden? How did you accept support without proving you couldn't manage your own life?

The thoughts were familiar. The same spiral he'd been working to untangle all year. The belief that needing anything meant fundamental inadequacy. That receiving help was evidence of weakness rather than evidence of being human. That independence was the only proof of worth.

"Every offer of help feels like an accusation," he said slowly, hearing the Co-Star theme land through his own words. "Like people are pointing out I can't handle things alone. So I'd rather struggle than appear weak. But that's—" He stopped. Tried to name what was actually happening. "That's not what people are offering. They're offering connection. And I'm hearing judgment."

"The walls you've built keep nothing safe," Orvenna said. "They just keep you isolated. Which you already know. So what are you actually protecting?"

Sefiathan watched wind move across the frozen marsh. Felt the question land deeper than his usual defenses could reach. What was he protecting? Not his autonomy—pushing people away didn't make him more independent. Not his worth—isolation didn't prove he could handle things alone. Not his safety—the walls didn't prevent harm, they just ensured he'd face difficulty without support.

He was protecting the story. The narrative where he was capable and self-sufficient and didn't need anyone. Where accepting help meant failure. Where dependency was the worst possible outcome. The story that let him pretend he could control whether people saw him as weak by simply not letting them close enough to witness his struggles.

But the story was smoke. Had no substance beyond what he kept feeding it. And maintaining it cost him actual connection with people who were reaching out not because they saw him as weak but because they saw him as someone worth knowing. Someone worth including. Someone whose company they genuinely wanted.

Wendell hadn't offered companionship as charity. He'd offered it as friendship. And Sefiathan had heard accusation where there was only invitation.

The recognition hurt. Not dramatically but with the dull ache of realizing you've been doing something self-sabotaging for so long you forgot it was optional. He'd spent three days avoiding someone who cared about him. Three days choosing isolation over the risk of appearing dependent. Three days feeding smoke and calling it protection.

"I need to talk to him," Sefiathan said.

"You need to let people reach you," Orvenna corrected. "Not just Wendell. But anyone who's offering connection instead of judgment. Your fear of appearing dependent is pushing away everyone who might actually matter. Which leaves you with exactly what you're afraid of—handling everything alone not because you're capable but because you've made yourself unavailable."

The words landed with precision. This was the pattern. The thing he kept doing in new variations. Building walls and calling it boundaries. Choosing isolation and calling it independence. Pushing people away and calling it protection. The mechanisms were familiar even when the specific circumstances changed.

He found Wendell near the edge of the gathering, packing supplies for tomorrow's departure. The healer looked up when Sefiathan approached but his expression was careful. Neutral. Not cold but not openly warm either. The expression of someone who'd been pushed away and wasn't sure whether reaching back was welcome.

"I'm sorry," Sefiathan said. Then corrected himself, remembering the lessons about specific apologies. "I'm sorry I avoided you. That wasn't about you. That was about me being afraid accepting your company would make me look dependent."

Wendell set down the pack he'd been organizing. "I wasn't offering charity."

"I know. But I heard it that way anyway. Heard accusation where you were offering friendship." Sefiathan paused. "I do that. Assume every offer of help is evidence I can't handle things alone. So I push people away rather than risk appearing weak. Which is—" He stopped. Found the courage to name it plainly. "Which is stupid. Because it means I lose connection with people who matter while protecting against judgment that only exists in my head."

Something shifted in Wendell's expression. Not quite forgiveness but recognition. Like he'd seen this pattern before in other people. "You can need things and still be capable. Those aren't mutually exclusive."

"I'm learning that. Slowly."

Wendell smiled. "Well. Learned it fast enough to come talk to me before I left. That counts for something."

They stood together in comfortable quiet. Around them the gathering continued its rhythms. Orvenna was working with another student. Korrin was laughing with the young centaurs. Calantha was helping someone repair a tent damaged in yesterday's wind. Life happening in its ordinary complexity. People reaching for each other not because anyone was weak but because that's what people did. Connection as basic requirement rather than emergency measure.

The walls kept nothing safe. The fear of appearing dependent was just isolation wearing a protective name. And the people reaching out didn't see dependency—they saw someone worth knowing. Worth including. Worth the ordinary effort of friendship.

Worth working on. Worth the discomfort of receiving care without turning it into evidence of inadequacy. Worth learning that needing things was human, not weakness.

The smoke had no substance. He could step through it. Could let people reach him. Could accept that connection wasn't accusation.

The morning was cold and bright and full of people who genuinely wanted his company.
