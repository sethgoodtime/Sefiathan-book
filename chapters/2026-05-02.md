# **Day 121 — May 2, 2026**

The descent took three days just to reach the treeline. Orvenna set the pace—glacially slow, stopping every few hours to check Sefiathan's extremities, to force warm broth down his throat, to assess whether his body was stable enough to continue. The grey-haired man was named Wendell, a healer from a village south of the Frostmere who'd been visiting the gathering when Korrin asked for help. The younger woman was his daughter Maeve, who had her father's gift for reading bodies in crisis and none of his patience for people who pushed themselves to the edge of death chasing visions.

"Reckless," she said on the second day, checking his feet for frostbite damage. "Absolutely reckless. You're lucky you still have all your toes."

Sefiathan didn't argue. Couldn't argue, really—his voice was still mostly gone, reduced to hoarse whispers that hurt to produce. But also didn't want to argue. She was right. He'd been reckless. Had prioritized being right about the call over the practical reality that bodies need food and warmth and preparation. Had almost died because he'd been so certain his instincts mattered more than basic survival logistics.

The problem—the thing he kept turning over in his depleted mind as they descended—was that he didn't regret going. Regretted the carelessness, yes. Regretted not telling anyone his plans. Regretted pushing past reasonable limits without proper supplies. But the journey itself? The twenty-six days climbing toward something that recognized him? The moment at the edge of the fracture when the darkness sang his melody back? He couldn't regret that even knowing how close he'd come to dying.

Which meant he still hadn't learned the lesson. Or was learning it sideways. Or was discovering that some lessons couldn't be learned through better planning—they required the actual collision between certainty and consequence to understand what needed adjusting.

On the third day they reached proper forest. The temperature was warmer here, the air less thin, his body marginally more functional. They made camp in a clearing where spring was already asserting itself—early flowers pushing through last year's leaf litter, birds making territorial claims in the canopy above. Wendell built a fire while Maeve prepared food. Orvenna sat beside Sefiathan and watched him with that patient attention that suggested she was waiting for something specific to surface.

"Your voice is coming back," she said finally. "Which means you can tell me why you went alone."

Sefiathan tested his throat. Found words possible even if they still hurt. "Didn't plan to go alone. Just started walking and didn't stop."

"For twenty-six days."

"The pull was strong."

"Strong enough to die for?"

He didn't answer immediately. The fire crackled. Somewhere in the trees a bird was practicing variations on a three-note pattern that sounded uncomfortably similar to his melody. Finally: "I don't know. Maybe. It felt like if I didn't follow it, I'd spend the rest of my life wondering what I'd missed."

"And now you know," Orvenna said. "You missed physical completion. Reached the threshold but couldn't descend. So was it worth nearly dying? Worth making Korrin worry? Worth three people dropping everything to track you into the mountains and drag you back?"

The questions landed with precision. Not accusatory. Just direct. She wanted actual answers, not comfortable deflection. Sefiathan watched the fire and tried to find truth that accounted for all the contradictions.

"I was right about needing to go," he said slowly. "The recognition was real. Whatever's in that fracture knew me specifically. Called me with intention. I'm not sorry I followed that. But I was careless about how I followed it. Didn't think about the people who'd worry. Didn't prepare properly. Didn't consider that being right about the call didn't justify the reckless pursuit."

"And if you could do it again? Knowing what you know now?"

"I'd tell someone my plans. Bring proper supplies. Maybe ask if anyone wanted to come with me." He paused. "But I'd still go. The pull mattered. Even if I couldn't complete the descent. Even if arrival just meant reaching the edge. That recognition—something vast seeing me clearly and calling me forward—I needed to answer that. Needed to know I was capable of following my instincts that far."

Orvenna nodded like he'd finally said something worth working with. "So the lesson isn't that you were wrong to go. It's that certainty without tact costs you allies. You can be right and still lose people. Can have accurate instincts and still damage relationships. Can follow your truth and still need to account for the people who care whether you survive."

It was exactly what she'd said three days ago when they'd found him barely conscious at the fracture's edge. But hearing it now—after three days of slow descent, after his body had stabilized enough for his mind to process beyond immediate survival—it landed differently. Less like criticism. More like instruction. Like she was teaching him the same thing Orvenna always taught: how to listen to what the world was actually saying instead of what he wanted it to mean.

"Korrin was angry," she continued. "Not that you went. That you didn't tell him you were going. He thought you'd died in the mountains. Spent two weeks imagining finding your frozen body. And then when I told him I'd located you alive but barely, he felt guilty for being angry because obviously you'd been through something terrible. So now he's carrying both the worry and the guilt and the unresolved anger, and you're going to have to deal with that when we get back."

Sefiathan closed his eyes. The weight of it was familiar—other people's feelings about his choices, the complex web of hurt and care and frustration that came from disappearing without explanation. He'd spent years trying to minimize his impact, trying to not be a burden, trying to justify his existence through adequate performance. And then he'd spent months learning he could take up space without apology. But somewhere in that learning he'd overcorrected. Had started treating his right to exist fully as license to pursue his truth without accounting for how that pursuit affected people who cared about him.

Being right didn't mean people had to accept the careless pursuit. Taking up space didn't mean ignoring the space others occupied. Following your instincts didn't exempt you from considering the people who'd worry if you vanished into mountains for a month.

"I need to apologize," he said.

"You need to own the impact," Orvenna corrected. "Not apologize for existing. Not apologize for following the call. But own that your carelessness had consequences for people who care about you. That's different from apologizing for taking up space. That's acknowledging that space includes other people and relationships require mutual consideration."

The distinction mattered. He'd spent so long apologizing for existing that the idea of apologizing for anything felt like regression. But this wasn't apologizing for being too much or needing things or following his truth. This was acknowledging that his choices had impact beyond himself. That being certain about his instincts didn't mean those instincts existed in isolation. That relationships required accounting for the other person's experience, not just justifying his own.

Maeve brought food—actual food, solid and warm and more than the broth they'd been forcing into him for days. Sefiathan ate slowly, feeling his body remember what satiation felt like. Around them the forest settled into evening sounds. The fire burned steady. His voice was coming back. His strength was coming back. And with them, the responsibility to deal with what his careless certainty had cost.

Tomorrow they'd reach the Frostmere gathering. Tomorrow he'd face Korrin's complicated anger-worry-guilt. Tomorrow he'd have to own the impact of his choices without apologizing for the choices themselves. The challenge would be holding both truths simultaneously—that he'd been right to follow the call and careless about how he'd followed it. That his instincts mattered and so did the people who cared whether he survived. That taking up space required considering the space others occupied too.

Worth working on. Worth the uncomfortable conversation ahead. Worth learning that certainty without tact was just isolation wearing a different name.

His voice was coming back. Time to use it with more care.
