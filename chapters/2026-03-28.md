**Day 86 — March 28, 2026**

The shift happened so gradually Sefiathan almost missed it. He was helping Korrin reinforce tent stakes against an incoming storm when he realized he'd stopped measuring himself against everyone else. Stopped comparing his struggles to their apparent ease. Stopped treating their competence as evidence of his inadequacy.

It was such a small thing. Korrin worked methodically, his centaur strength making quick work of driving stakes deep into frozen ground. A month ago, Sefiathan would have turned that into a whole internal narrative about his own weakness, his human limitations, his fundamental insufficiency compared to someone who just naturally functioned better. Would have added it to the catalogue of ways he didn't measure up.

But this morning he just watched Korrin work and felt grateful for the help. Noticed the efficiency without making it about his own deficiency. Saw Korrin's competence as just what it was—Korrin being good at a thing—rather than proof that Sefiathan was broken in comparison.

The voice in his head that usually provided running commentary on his inadequacies had gone quiet. Not permanently, probably. But quiet enough that he noticed the absence.

They finished with the stakes and Korrin straightened, surveying their work with satisfaction. "Storm should hit by evening. These'll hold."

"Thank you for helping." Sefiathan heard the simplicity in his own words. Not apologizing for needing help. Not explaining why he couldn't do it alone. Just gratitude, clean and uncomplicated.

"That's what I'm here for." Korrin glanced at him with something like approval. "You're different lately. Less apologetic. More just present."

Sefiathan considered that. "I think I stopped comparing myself to everyone else. Stopped treating other people's competence as evidence that I'm malfunctioning."

"About time." Korrin's tone was matter-of-fact. "You've been measuring yourself against an imaginary standard of proper functioning that doesn't actually exist. Just exhausting yourself trying to match up to people who aren't even in the same race."

They walked back toward the main gathering area. The wind was picking up, carrying the smell of incoming weather. People were securing their camps, moving with the practiced efficiency of those who'd weathered storms before. Sefiathan watched them without the usual mental tally of how much better they were at this than him. Just saw people doing what needed doing, each in their own way, with their own strengths and struggles.

It was freeing, this absence of constant comparison. Like setting down weight he hadn't realized he'd been carrying. The voice that had spent years telling him he wasn't attractive enough, successful enough, competent enough, normal enough—that voice had built its case entirely on measuring him against everyone else and finding him lacking. But what if the measuring itself was the problem? What if there was no singular standard of proper functioning that everyone else had mastered except him?

What if people were just different, and that was fine?

Calantha found him near the fire later, settling beside him with two cups of something hot and spiced. She handed him one without speaking, content in the quiet. They sat watching the gathering prepare for the storm—tents being reinforced, supplies being moved to protected areas, people helping each other with the work of weathering difficult conditions together.

"Orvenna wants to see you," Calantha said eventually. "Said something about you finally getting out of your own way enough to learn properly."

Sefiathan felt that observation settle. He had been in his own way, hadn't he? So busy measuring himself against others, cataloguing his deficiencies, treating his struggles as evidence of fundamental inadequacy that he'd barely had attention left for actually learning anything. The endless appraisal had consumed energy that could have gone toward growth, connection, presence.

He found Orvenna at the wind-listening stones, standing with her usual economy of motion, face turned toward the incoming storm with expression of deep attention.

"You're different," she observed without preamble. "Less static in your attention. Less of that constant measuring that was drowning out everything else."

"I stopped comparing myself to everyone." Sefiathan joined her at the stones. "Stopped treating other people's competence as proof of my inadequacy."

"Good. That measuring was just noise. Made it impossible to hear what the wind was actually saying because you were too busy hearing your own inadequacy." She gestured toward the stones. "Listen now. Without the comparison. Without the appraisal. Just listen to what's there."

Sefiathan settled into stillness. Let the wind move through him without interpretation, without judgment, without measuring his ability to hear it against anyone else's. Just present with the sound, with the movement, with the particular quality of air that came before storms.

And something shifted. The wind wasn't just sound anymore. It was information. About pressure changes and temperature gradients and the movement of weather systems across landscape. About the mood of the gathering, the collective nervous energy of people preparing for difficult conditions. About something deeper—that old pull he'd felt before, the resonance that lived under the surface of things.

"There it is," Orvenna said quietly. "That's your particular way of hearing. Not better or worse than anyone else's. Just yours. You couldn't access it properly before because you were too busy measuring whether you were doing it right."

Sefiathan opened his eyes. "I've been comparing myself to an imaginary standard that doesn't exist."

"Most people do. Spend their whole lives measuring themselves against others and feeling inadequate because they're trying to be something they're not." She turned to face him fully. "You know what makes you compelling? What makes you worth paying attention to?"

"What?"

"You see the world differently. Always have. That sideways attention you bring to things, that strange intensity, that way you notice what others miss—that's what makes you interesting. Not despite being odd. Because of it." She paused. "But you spent so long trying to measure up to some imaginary normal that you almost strangled that difference. Almost convinced yourself it was deficiency rather than gift."

The words landed with uncomfortable accuracy. How many years had he spent trying to be normal? Trying to function like everyone else? Trying to eliminate the strangeness that made him feel perpetually out of step with the world? Treating his particular way of moving through life as something that needed correction rather than something that might be valuable exactly as it was?

"The voice in my head that says I'm not attractive or successful enough," Sefiathan said slowly. "It's been measuring me against everyone else and building a case for my inadequacy based on comparison. But the comparison itself is the problem. I'm not supposed to be like everyone else."

"No one is." Orvenna's voice held something like satisfaction. "But most people spend their lives trying anyway. You're finally stopping. Finally recognizing that your particular way of being has value. That the strangeness isn't deficiency."

Thunder rumbled in the distance. The storm was getting closer. Sefiathan felt the wind shift, felt the change in pressure that meant rain would follow soon. Felt it all without measuring whether he was feeling it correctly, whether his perception matched some imaginary standard of proper wind-reading.

He thought about personality. About how he'd spent so long trying to compensate for not having conventional attractiveness or obvious success by trying to be useful, helpful, accommodating. Trying to be worth people's time through service rather than presence. But what if his personality—his strange, intense, sideways way of engaging with the world—was actually more interesting than any of that?

What if people who genuinely cared about him weren't measuring him against conventional standards at all? Weren't comparing his attractiveness to magazine covers or his success to cultural benchmarks? What if they just found him compelling because of how he saw things differently, noticed what others missed, brought that particular intensity to everything he did?

Calantha had said something about that once. Called him interesting. Not despite his complications but because of them. Had seemed genuinely curious about his internal experience rather than wishing he'd just be simpler.

The first drops of rain began to fall. Orvenna moved toward shelter without urgency, trusting the weather to do what weather did. Sefiathan followed, feeling something settle in his chest. Something like permission to stop measuring. To stop comparing. To stop treating his particular way of being as something that needed justification or improvement.

He was compelling because he saw differently. Because he brought strange intensity to things. Because he noticed what others missed and cared about what he noticed with unreasonable depth. That wasn't inadequacy. That was just who he was.

And people who loved him wouldn't want him to be anyone else.

The storm broke fully as he reached his tent. Rain hammered against canvas. Wind howled through the gathering. But Sefiathan felt warm inside, sheltered not just by fabric but by the realization that he'd been measuring himself against an imaginary standard that had nothing to do with actual worth.

He didn't need to be conventionally attractive or obviously successful. Didn't need to function like everyone else or match some imaginary normal. He just needed to be himself—strange and intense and seeing sideways—and let that be enough.

The voice in his head that had spent years building cases for his inadequacy had finally gone quiet.

And in the silence, he could hear the wind singing through stones, could feel the resonance that lived under everything, could sense his own particular way of moving through the world without apologizing for it.

That was compelling enough.
