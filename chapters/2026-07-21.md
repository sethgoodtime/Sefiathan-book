# **Day 201 — July 21, 2026**

Sefiathan woke to the reading's instruction sitting sharp in his chest: Stop putting yourself last. Not gentle suggestion. Direct confrontation with the pattern he'd been running since yesterday's recognition that his light was inexhaustible. Because knowing the light couldn't be depleted didn't mean he'd stopped treating it like something less important than everyone else's needs. Didn't mean he'd stopped defaulting to the back of every line, the last consideration, the afterthought in his own life.

The excavation camp was stirring into morning routine and Sefiathan lay in his bedroll recognizing the shape of the past three days. He'd understood his light was inexhaustible. Had stopped covering it with apology and performance. But he'd immediately channeled that understanding back into service—waking more warming stones, helping with excavation work, making sure everyone else was comfortable before considering his own needs. Same pattern. Different justification. Still putting himself last.

The reading said it plainly: You've been choosing to be agreeable over being honest about what you need. Sefiathan felt that land. Yesterday he'd wanted to rest but helped Petran excavate a new chamber instead. The day before he'd been hungry but waited until everyone else had eaten before taking food. This morning he was thirsty but the water barrels were across camp and scholars were already gathered there talking and he didn't want to interrupt their conversation just for his own need.

Stop putting yourself last. The instruction felt almost aggressive in its directness. Not "consider yourself more." Not "balance your needs with others." Just stop. Stop treating your needs as afterthought. Stop making yourself the last priority. Stop being so agreeable that you disappear into service and call it generosity.

He got up and walked directly to the water barrels where three scholars were deep in discussion about glaze patterns on pottery shards. Filled his cup without waiting for a pause in conversation. Drank it standing there. Filled it again. Thessaly caught his eye and smiled slightly—recognition of something shifting. He wasn't interrupting. Wasn't apologizing. Was just getting water because he needed water.

That simple act felt enormous. Felt like violation of some unwritten rule that his needs shouldn't disrupt other people's flow. But whose rule was that? Who had decided his thirst mattered less than an uninterrupted conversation about pottery? He had. Through years of making himself small and convenient and unobtrusive. Through decades of choosing agreeable over honest. Through a lifetime of putting himself last and calling it courtesy.

Calantha found him eating breakfast alone—another small rebellion. Usually he waited to eat with others, made it social, turned even basic sustenance into opportunity to be agreeable and pleasant company. But this morning he was hungry so he ate. Alone. Without waiting for the right moment or the right company.

"You look determined," she said, settling beside him with her own food.

"The reading said stop putting yourself last. I've been doing that my whole life. Even here. Even after recognizing my light is inexhaustible. I just turned it into better service instead of actually prioritizing my own needs."

"What do you need?"

The question hit different today. Not abstract. Not philosophical. Practical and immediate. What did he actually need right now, in this moment, in this place? "I need to leave. Need to move on. The scholars pack up in two weeks but I need to go sooner. I've been staying because leaving feels selfish. Because they're used to having warming stones now and I don't want to take that comfort away. But I'm ready to go and I keep putting that need last."

Saying it out loud made it real. Made it true. He was ready to leave the excavation site. Had been ready for days but kept choosing to be agreeable instead of honest. Kept prioritizing their comfort over his own need for movement. Kept making himself last in the hierarchy of whose needs mattered.

Calantha was quiet for a moment. "So leave."

"Just like that?"

"Just like that. They'll figure out the warming stones. Or they'll be cold at night. Either way, it's not your job to stay past when you're ready just because leaving inconveniences them."

The truth of that sat heavy. He'd been treating his readiness to move as less important than their preference for warm nights. Had been choosing agreeable—staying, being helpful, maintaining the comfortable status quo—over honest—acknowledging he was done here and needed to go. Had been putting himself last in his own journey.

The Do list felt pointed. Big Lebowski. Black nail polish. Disco. All things that took up space unapologetically. That existed loudly and confidently without shrinking to accommodate others. Sefiathan had spent so long being quietly agreeable that the idea of being loud felt foreign. Felt selfish. Felt like taking up space he hadn't earned.

But that was the pattern talking. That was the automatic default to last place. His needs weren't selfish just because meeting them inconvenienced others. His readiness to move wasn't less important than their preference for him to stay. His life wasn't meant to be organized around making everyone else comfortable.

Petran approached around midday asking if he'd help excavate another chamber. Sefiathan felt the automatic yes forming. The agreeable response. The putting-himself-last pattern that made him available for whatever anyone needed. But he caught it. Held it. Let it sit unspoken while he actually checked what he wanted.

"No," he said. Simple. Direct. "I'm leaving tomorrow morning. I need to pack and prepare and I'm not excavating today."

Petran looked surprised—not offended, just surprised that Sefiathan had said no. "Tomorrow? But we have two more weeks here."

"You have two more weeks. I'm ready to go now. I've been staying to be agreeable but that's me putting myself last again. So I'm leaving tomorrow."

The words felt strange in his mouth. Felt bold and direct and unapologetic in a way he wasn't used to inhabiting. But they also felt true. Felt like actually prioritizing his own needs instead of making them conditional on whether they fit conveniently into everyone else's plans.

The afternoon stretched hot and Sefiathan packed his belongings. Not rushing. Not performing departure. Just actually preparing to leave because he was ready to leave and that was enough reason. He didn't need permission. Didn't need everyone to agree it was the right time. Didn't need to justify his readiness with elaborate explanations. Just needed to stop putting his own movement last in the hierarchy of what mattered.

Thessaly helped him organize supplies. "Where are you going?"

"I don't know yet. But I know I need to move. Need to keep the journey going instead of settling into another comfortable stop that's really just hiding with better justification."

"That's honest."

"Yeah. Finally. I've been so focused on being agreeable that I forgot honest was an option. Forgot I could just say what I needed without apologizing for it or making sure everyone else was comfortable first."

The Don't list made sense now. Ego. Floppy hat. Fast dance. All the ways he performed confidence instead of actually inhabiting his own needs. The ego that made every decision about how he appeared to others. The performative casualness that hid real needs behind breezy agreement. The rushing that avoided actually stating what he wanted clearly.

Evening settled cool and Sefiathan sat with the scholars one last time around warming stones that would stay active for weeks after he left. Proof his contribution existed whether he was here to maintain it or not. Proof his presence wasn't required for the work to continue. Proof leaving didn't erase what he'd done.

Calantha raised her cup. "To Sefiathan, who's finally learning to put himself first sometimes."

"Not first," he corrected. "Just not last. Not afterthought. Not the final consideration after everyone else has been accommodated."

The distinction mattered. He wasn't trying to become selfish or demanding or inconsiderate. Just trying to stop treating his own needs as inherently less important than everyone else's. Trying to stop choosing agreeable over honest when those two things conflicted. Trying to let his readiness to move matter as much as anyone's preference for him to stay.

That night Sefiathan lay in his bedroll for the last time at the excavation site and felt the shift settling. Tomorrow he'd leave. Not because he had perfect plans. Not because he knew exactly where he was going or what came next. Just because he was ready and that was enough. His readiness mattered. His needs mattered. His journey mattered enough to keep moving even when staying was more agreeable.

Stop putting yourself last. Simple instruction. Revolutionary practice. He'd spent twenty-seven years defaulting to back of every line, treating his needs as optional extras that only got met if they didn't inconvenience anyone. But that wasn't courtesy. Wasn't generosity. Was just another way of apologizing for existing. Another way of making himself small enough to be tolerable.

The desert stars appeared overhead, vast and indifferent and beautiful. Tomorrow he'd wake and pack and leave while the scholars were still sleeping. Not to avoid goodbyes—just because morning movement felt right and he wasn't going to delay it to be agreeable. His light was inexhaustible. His needs mattered. His readiness counted as reason enough.

For the first time in his life, Sefiathan was putting himself somewhere other than last. Not first. Not demanding priority. Just equal. Just mattering as much as anyone else's comfort or preference or plan. Just honest about what he needed instead of automatically choosing agreeable. The revolution felt quiet but complete.
