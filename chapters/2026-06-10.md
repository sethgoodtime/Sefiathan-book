# **Day 160 — June 10, 2026**

The shift wasn't dramatic. Sefiathan didn't wake up suddenly enlightened about accepting good things or magically cured of his tendency to brace against pleasure. But something had loosened yesterday—some small internal permission to believe that what was happening might actually be better than what came before. That the present didn't have to be constantly measured against past disappointments. That forward could mean improvement instead of just different versions of the same problems.

He'd been sitting with the dying tree again this morning, watching dawn light catch on the expanded patch of healthier bark. The growth was slow but undeniable now—maybe a third of the trunk showing subtle signs of recovery. Not dramatic healing but steady remembering. The tree learning to be alive again without constantly preparing for collapse.

Orvenna had joined him without speaking, settling into her usual crouch where she could feel underground currents. They sat in comfortable silence for a while before she finally said, "The resonance is working better."

"I'm not doing anything different."

"Yes you are. You've stopped treating it like it might betray you." She touched the healthier bark with one weathered finger. "Been watching you with this tree for weeks now. At first you kept checking whether the healing was real. Whether it would last. Whether you were doing it wrong. Now you just sit and let it happen. Trust that what's growing is actually growth."

The observation landed with uncomfortable accuracy. He had been doing that—constantly second-guessing whether the tree's improvement was real or temporary or about to reverse itself. Treating every sign of healing like it might be a false positive. But sometime in the past few days he'd stopped cataloging evidence and just started accepting that the tree was getting better because it was getting better. No hidden trap. No impending reversal. Just actual improvement.

"I think I've been treating everything that way," he said quietly. "Measuring the present against the past. Waiting for good things to reveal themselves as secretly bad. Never quite believing that what's happening now could actually be better than what came before."

"That's exhausting."

"Yeah."

Orvenna stood, brushed bark dust from her hands. "The work you're doing matters. Not because it's fixing broken things but because you're learning to trust that healing is real when it happens. That growth doesn't have to justify itself. That better can just mean better without constantly proving it against worse."

She left him there and Sefiathan sat with her words as the morning warmed. The thing about believing the present could be better than the past was that it required letting go of the past as a measuring stick. He'd spent so much time cataloging his grievances, his disappointments, his evidence of unworthiness. Had built an entire internal museum of why things would always go wrong eventually. And every good thing that happened in the present got immediately compared against that collection of historical pain—tested for authenticity, measured for permanence, checked for hidden catches.

But what if he just stopped doing that? What if the fact that he'd been hurt before didn't mean he'd be hurt again? What if his childhood making him feel inadequate didn't mean his present had to confirm that inadequacy? What if Mira's relationship ending badly didn't mean Calantha's friendship would also turn sour? What if his father's dismissiveness didn't mean Orvenna's teaching would eventually reveal itself as secretly judgmental?

The past had happened. The pain had been real. But using it as constant evidence for future betrayal was just choosing to live in old hurt instead of present possibility.

Korrin found him mid-morning with news that a messenger had arrived at the gathering. Not urgent but interesting—word from one of the southern settlements that they'd found some kind of ancient ruin in the Wandering Dunes. Something about architecture that shouldn't exist in that region. Some scholars were organizing an expedition to investigate and they were looking for anyone with unusual abilities who might want to join.

"Thought you might be interested," Korrin said, settling beside him with the easy comfort of genuine friendship. "Given the whole resonance thing. Maybe your attention could help wake up whatever's sleeping there."

Sefiathan's first instinct was familiar—all the reasons it wouldn't work. He wasn't trained enough yet. The resonance was too unpredictable. He'd probably just get in the way of actual scholars who knew what they were doing. Better to stay at the Frostmere where Orvenna could keep teaching him until he was competent enough to be useful elsewhere.

But underneath that automatic deflection was something else. Curiosity. Actual interest in seeing ancient ruins in the desert. Genuine excitement about the possibility of his attention waking something that had been sleeping for who knows how long. Not performing excitement or trying to convince himself he should feel it—just real enthusiasm about something that sounded genuinely interesting.

"When does the expedition leave?" he asked.

"Two weeks. They're gathering in Kethril first, then heading south together." Korrin studied him with the directness he'd come to appreciate. "You don't have to decide now. Just thought you should know it's an option."

After Korrin left, Sefiathan sat with the possibility. Two weeks wasn't much time but it also wasn't immediate. Enough space to think without pressure. Enough room to consider whether this was something he actually wanted or just another opportunity to prove his worth through usefulness.

The difference mattered. He'd spent most of his life saying yes to things because he thought he should. Because refusing might mean disappointing someone. Because being helpful was how he earned the right to exist around people. But lately he'd been learning that genuine interest felt different from obligatory helpfulness. Real enthusiasm had a lightness to it. A pull toward rather than a push from guilt or duty.

This felt like pull. Like actual curiosity about ruins in the desert and what his resonance might wake there. Like genuine excitement about expanding beyond the Frostmere, not because he'd learned everything Orvenna could teach but because he was ready to practice in new contexts. Like trusting that what came next could be better than what came before because it was building on growth instead of running from inadequacy.

Calantha joined him for lunch, bringing news that she and her friend had spent the morning together just being weird without performing. "It was terrifying," she said with something close to joy. "But also better. Like actually better than when I was trying to be impressive. They kept laughing at my odd observations instead of looking confused. Kept adding their own strange thoughts instead of trying to redirect to normal conversation. I think they might actually like the real me better."

"What if they do?" Sefiathan asked.

"What?"

"What if this is actually better than before? What if you're not waiting for it to go bad but just experiencing it being good?"

Calantha considered this while eating. "I keep trying to measure it against the past. Against other relationships that didn't work. Against all the times I performed and still got rejected. But that's not fair to this. This isn't those other things. This is just what's happening now."

"Which could be better than what came before."

"Yeah." She smiled, something genuine and undefended. "It really could be."

The afternoon stretched long and warm. Sefiathan practiced with the tree again but this time Orvenna had him work with intention. Not forcing the healing but directing his attention with purpose. Noticing where the disease was strongest and choosing to sit with those parts. Trusting that his resonance would remind the dying sections how to live without him micromanaging the process.

It was harder than passive attention. Required making choices about where to focus instead of just letting his odd notice-everything tendency scatter everywhere. But it also felt powerful in a way that was new. Like learning that his attention could be both genuine and directed. That trusting his instincts didn't mean abandoning all structure but meant choosing with intention instead of performing what he thought was expected.

The diseased sections didn't heal dramatically but something shifted. Small changes in the bark texture. Subtle movements in the sap. Not reversal of death but pausing of decline. The tree remembering that life was an option even in the parts that had given up.

Evening came cool and clear. The gathering was preparing for a celebration tomorrow—some kind of seasonal marker the wind-readers observed. Sefiathan had been invited to play his melody if he wanted. Not performing for approval but sharing because people genuinely enjoyed it. Because the song mattered not as proof of his worth but as actual contribution to communal joy.

He was learning to tell the difference. Learning that what came next could be better than what came before without constantly proving itself against historical pain. Learning that the present could just be the present—good or difficult or strange—without constant comparison to past disappointment.

The tree beside him was more alive than it had been yesterday and tomorrow there would be celebration and in two weeks there might be ruins in the desert and none of it had to justify itself against what came before. Just forward. Just better. Just trusting that growth was real when it happened.
