# **Day 180 — June 30, 2026**

The morning broke clear and Sefiathan woke with the reading's words already circling his mind. It's not rocket science. Stop turning minor decisions into federal cases. The answer you need is obvious and has been staring you in the face.

He'd been doing it again. Overcomplicating. Building elaborate theories about what the ruins meant, what his magic was for, what he was supposed to learn here. Constructing complex frameworks to understand simple truth. Making everything harder than it needed to be because somewhere deep down he believed that if the answer was obvious, it couldn't be real. Couldn't be enough. Couldn't possibly be the actual solution.

The excavation site was already busy when he arrived. Petran waved him over to continue work on the water channels, but Sefiathan found himself stopping at the edge of the main courtyard instead. Looking at the layout they'd uncovered. The pattern that had been obvious from the first day but that everyone—including him—had been analyzing and theorizing about instead of just seeing.

The entire complex was designed around the central warming stones. Everything else—the water channels, the building placement, the carved spirals—all of it pointed back to those pillars. Not metaphorically. Literally. The channels directed water toward them. The buildings created sheltered spaces around them. The spirals marked sightlines to them from every major structure. This wasn't some elaborate mystery requiring careful archaeological analysis. It was straightforward functional design. The warming stones were the point. Everything else existed to support them.

Sefiathan had been treating this like a puzzle that needed solving when it was actually just architecture doing exactly what it looked like it was doing. Keeping people warm in the desert cold. Providing heat. Offering comfort. The simplest, most obvious purpose imaginable. He'd been so busy looking for deeper meaning that he'd missed the truth staring him in the face for three weeks.

Thessaly found him standing there and followed his gaze. "You're seeing something."

"I've been overcomplicating it. The warming stones are the whole purpose. Not part of some larger system. Not a clue to deeper mysteries. Just stones that kept people warm. That's what this place was for."

"Yes. We established that in the first week."

"But I kept looking for more. Kept thinking there had to be something complex and hidden. Kept making it rocket science when it was just obvious practical design."

Thessaly studied him with that assessing look. "You do that a lot. Make simple things complicated."

"Yeah. Because simple feels like I'm missing something. Like the real answer must be harder to find."

"Why?"

The question landed hard. Why did he assume truth had to be complex? Why did obvious answers feel insufficient? Why did he keep building elaborate theories when the simple explanation was right there, working exactly as designed, not hiding anything?

Because his whole life he'd been told he was missing the point. That the obvious answer was too simple for him to grasp. That if it seemed straightforward, he must be misunderstanding. His father had done it constantly—taking Sefiathan's simple observations and making them complicated, turning clear truth into convoluted explanations, suggesting that Sefiathan's direct perception was naive. Missing nuance. Failing to see the real complexity adults understood.

So Sefiathan had learned to distrust the obvious. Had learned to assume that what he saw directly couldn't be the actual truth. Had learned to build elaborate frameworks and theories because surely the real answer was hidden somewhere deeper, somewhere he couldn't quite reach with his inadequate perception.

But the ruins didn't work that way. The warming stones heated the buildings because that was their entire purpose. The water channels directed rainfall because water needed directing. The carved spirals marked sightlines because people needed to navigate. Simple functional design solving simple practical problems. No hidden meanings. No elaborate mysteries. Just people building what they needed to survive and adding beauty because beauty mattered to them.

Trust what you see, not what you suspect. The reading had it exactly right. Sefiathan had been suspecting complexity, building theories, imagining hidden depths. When what he was seeing—warming stones that warmed, water channels that channeled water, marks of love that marked love—that was the actual truth. Right there. Obvious.

He walked to the nearest warming stone and sat with his back against it. The heat was gentle and steady. Not dramatic. Not magical in flashy ways. Just consistently warm in a place that got cold at night. Doing exactly what it appeared to be doing. No federal case required.

Calantha found him there an hour later. "You look different."

"I've been making everything too complicated. Turning minor decisions into major dilemmas. The answer's usually obvious."

"What brought that on?"

"The warming stones. They're just warming stones. They warm things. That's what they do. I kept looking for deeper purpose when the purpose was literally in the name."

She laughed. "People are simpler than you think."

"Yeah. The reading said that. Trust what you see, not what you suspect. I've been suspecting conspiracy when people were just being straightforward."

It was true about more than the ruins. He'd been doing it with the scholars too. Suspecting they were judging him when they were actually just working. Building theories about their disappointment when they were actually just focused on excavation. Making their simple actions into elaborate emotional plots because surely the truth of how they felt about him had to be more complex than what they showed on their faces.

But Petran had been straightforward. Had seen Sefiathan struggling with technique and shown him the right way. Simple exchange. No hidden agenda. Calantha had brought him water because he looked thirsty. Not as some complex gesture requiring interpretation. Just basic human care. Maren had asked him to help because she needed an extra set of hands. Not as a test or judgment. Just practical need for assistance.

People really were simpler than he thought. Most of the time they meant exactly what they said. Showed exactly what they felt. Did exactly what their actions suggested. The elaborate theories about hidden meanings and secret judgments—those were his additions. His complications. His refusal to trust the obvious truth right in front of him.

Drop the elaborate theories. Sefiathan sat with that instruction like it was medicine. He'd been building theories his whole life. Theories about why his father dismissed him. Theories about what his family really thought. Theories about why he was inadequate. Complex frameworks to explain simple painful truths he didn't want to face directly.

But what if he just trusted what he saw? His father was dismissive because that was how he treated people who didn't meet his standards. His family found him odd because he was odd and they didn't understand oddness. He felt inadequate because he'd spent twenty-seven years believing he was inadequate. Simple painful obvious truths. No elaborate theory required.

The warming stone stayed warm against his back and Sefiathan let himself feel how uncomplicated that was. Heat. Comfort. Stone doing what stone was designed to do. His magic had woken these stones not through some elaborate mystical process but through simple sustained attention. Caring about them until they remembered they could be warm. Obvious. Straightforward. Not rocket science.

He'd been turning his whole journey into a federal case. Making every choice into some profound crossroads. Building elaborate frameworks for what his magic meant, what he was discovering, who he was becoming. When the truth was simpler. He was learning to trust himself. Learning to be sponge instead of stone. Learning that his oddness mattered. Simple truths he could have written on day one if he'd trusted what he saw instead of what he suspected.

Petran called for him around midday and Sefiathan joined the excavation work with different attention. Not looking for hidden meanings. Not building theories about what the sand patterns revealed about ancient psychology. Just clearing sand because sand needed clearing. Exposing channels because channels needed exposing. Simple work. Obvious purpose.

"You're less tense today," Petran observed.

"I'm trying to stop making everything complicated."

"Good. Most things aren't complicated. They just are what they are."

Simple truth from someone who saw simply. Sefiathan absorbed it like water. Let it settle without adding layers of interpretation. Petran meant what he said. The work was what it appeared to be. The ruins told their story through straightforward design. No conspiracy. No hidden test. Just archaeology happening exactly as archaeology happened.

The day continued warm and clear and Sefiathan stayed with the obvious truth of it. Hot sun. Careful work. Simple human exchange. No federal cases. No elaborate theories. Just what was actually happening, exactly as it appeared to be happening.

That night he wrote one line in his journal. The answer was obvious the whole time. I just didn't trust it.

Simple truth. No complication needed.
