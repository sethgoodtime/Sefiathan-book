# **Day 140 — May 21, 2026**

The problem wasn't that Sefiathan didn't know what he wanted. He'd spent the last month at the Frostmere getting clearer about that than he'd ever been. He wanted to understand the resonance magic Orvenna kept circling around without naming directly. He wanted to learn how his attention affected things—the way trees seemed to grow stronger near him, the way people discovered new abilities after spending time in his presence, the way the ancient thing beneath the ice had stirred when he'd started listening properly. He wanted to stop apologizing for the intensity of his focus and start directing it intentionally.

But knowing what he wanted and actually changing his behavior to pursue it were apparently two different things.

He was still showing up to practice with Orvenna at dawn every morning, still listening to the deep winds with the concentration she demanded, still doing all the surface-level work of being a dedicated student. But he was also still hedging. Still scaling back his questions when they felt too direct. Still accepting vague non-answers when she deflected. Still treating his own curiosity like it needed to be managed rather than followed.

The realization hit during breakfast when Calantha asked him a simple question: "What did Orvenna teach you this morning?"

"The usual," he said. "Listening for patterns in the deep currents. Trying to feel where underground winds converge."

"That's what you did. I asked what she taught you."

Sefiathan opened his mouth to respond and found he didn't have an answer. Because Orvenna hadn't actually taught him anything new in weeks. She'd had him doing the same practice over and over—sit still, listen deep, notice patterns. Valuable work, probably. Building foundational skills. But he'd started to suspect she was waiting for him to do something. To ask something. To break out of the polite student role and demand she stop being cryptic about what she clearly knew he was capable of.

But he hadn't. Because demanding felt presumptuous. Because breaking the routine she'd established felt disrespectful. Because what if he asked directly and she said no? What if he pushed for real teaching and she decided he wasn't ready, wasn't worth the effort, wasn't as promising as he'd hoped?

Better to keep doing what she asked. Better to be grateful for the attention she was already giving him. Better to wait until she decided on her own to teach him more, to trust he'd earned access to whatever knowledge she was withholding.

Except that was the same pattern he'd been working to untangle all year. Scaling back what he wanted because his methods for getting it felt uncomfortable. Shrinking his dreams to fit habits that weren't actually serving him. Treating discomfort as evidence he was doing something wrong rather than evidence he was doing something different.

"I don't know what she taught me," he admitted to Calantha. "I've been doing what she tells me to do but I haven't pushed her to explain why. Haven't asked what I'm building toward. Haven't demanded she be more direct about what she sees in me."

"Why not?"

"Because what if she gets annoyed? What if I'm supposed to figure it out myself and asking directly proves I'm not ready? What if—" He stopped. Heard himself spiraling into familiar territory. "What if she says no."

Calantha set down her bowl with deliberate care. "So you're letting fear of rejection keep you from actually pursuing what you want. You're being a polite student instead of an honest one. You're shrinking your dreams to fit methods that feel safe."

The Co-Star theme landed through her words with uncomfortable precision. Your behavior should change to meet your goals, not the other way around. He'd been doing exactly the opposite—maintaining comfortable routines, avoiding confrontation, waiting for permission he didn't actually need. Acting like his goals had to fit neatly into established patterns instead of being willing to break those patterns when they stopped serving him.

He found Orvenna near the frozen marsh after breakfast, standing in her usual spot where the wind came clean off the ice. She didn't acknowledge his approach but her stillness shifted slightly—the way she became present when someone was about to speak.

"I need to ask you something," Sefiathan said. No preamble. No careful building to the point. Just direct.

"Finally."

The single word stopped him. "You've been waiting for me to ask?"

"I've been waiting for you to stop performing good student and start demanding real teaching." Orvenna turned to face him fully. "You want to understand the resonance. What your attention does. Why things change around you. But you've been too polite to say that directly. Too worried I'll think you're presumptuous."

"You knew?"

"I've known since the first day you sat down and asked to learn. I can see it in you—the way everything you pay attention to starts waking up. But I needed you to stop waiting for permission to want what you want. Stop scaling back your goals because the direct path feels uncomfortable."

Sefiathan felt something click into place. "The practice you've been having me do—listening to deep winds—that wasn't the actual teaching, was it?"

"It was preparation. Building the capacity to notice subtle patterns. But the real work can't start until you're willing to admit what you're actually here for. Until you stop pretending you just want to be a competent wind-reader and start acknowledging you want to understand something much bigger."

The directness was startling. Also exactly what he'd needed. He'd been treating his own goals like they were somehow inappropriate. Like wanting to understand his strange magic was greedy or presumptuous or too much. Like he should be grateful for whatever scraps of knowledge Orvenna was willing to give him rather than demanding she meet him where he actually was.

"I want to understand what I am," he said. Felt the words land with weight because they were true. "I want to know why my attention makes things change. Why people discover new abilities after spending time around me. Why trees grow stronger where I've been. Why the thing under the ice responded to my melody. I want to stop accidentally affecting things and start doing it intentionally."

"There. That wasn't so hard."

"It felt hard."

"Because you've been taught wanting things directly is rude. That goals should fit politely into established routines. That changing your behavior to meet what you actually want is somehow wrong." Orvenna's expression was stern but not unkind. "But your behavior should change to meet your goals. Not the other way around. You want real teaching? Then stop being a polite student. Ask direct questions. Push back when I'm cryptic. Demand I explain what I'm seeing instead of waiting for me to volunteer it."

Sefiathan felt the familiar fear rise—what if being direct made her withdraw? What if demanding real teaching proved he wasn't ready for it? But underneath the fear was something stronger. The recognition that he'd been here before. Had been working all year to learn that taking up space wasn't presumptuous. That stating what he wanted wasn't rude. That his goals mattered enough to pursue them even when the path felt uncomfortable.

"The resonance," he said. "What is it exactly? What am I doing when things change around me?"

Orvenna smiled. Actually smiled, which Sefiathan had never seen her do. "You're a catalyst. Your attention—when you give it fully, when you pay attention with that odd sideways love you have—it wakes up potential that was already there. You don't give people magic. You help them remember they had it all along. You don't make trees grow. You remind them how to be fully themselves."

The explanation landed like something he'd been waiting his whole life to hear. "And you can teach me how to do that intentionally?"

"I can teach you how to direct it. How to notice when it's happening. How to work with it instead of against it. But only if you're willing to stop shrinking your dreams to fit habits that aren't working. Stop being polite when you need to be direct. Stop waiting for permission to want what you want."

The path forward felt messier than Sefiathan had hoped. Not a clean progression from ignorance to knowledge but a constant willingness to break routines that stopped serving him. To push past discomfort. To demand rather than wait. To let his behavior change to meet his goals instead of scaling back his goals to fit comfortable patterns.

Worth working on. Worth the discomfort of being direct. Worth learning that sometimes the dream was bigger than the method and you had to be willing to make noise, break routines, demand real teaching instead of polite placeholders.

The morning was cold and bright and Orvenna was finally ready to teach him what he'd been too polite to ask for.
