# **Day 157 — June 7, 2026**

The thing about love setting you free was that Sefiathan had spent most of his life treating it like a transaction. Someone did something kind and he owed them gratitude. Someone showed interest and he owed them perfect performance of friendship. Someone offered teaching and he owed them flawless studenthood. Love—or what passed for it in his understanding—had always come with invisible price tags he spent years trying to pay off.

But sitting with the dying tree yesterday had cracked something open. Not the tree itself, though that small patch of healthier bark was still there this morning when he'd checked. What had cracked was his understanding of what his attention actually did when he stopped trying to control it. When he stopped performing authenticity and just existed in his uncomfortable, odd-angled shape, the resonance happened naturally. Not because he'd earned it or done it correctly but because that's what his genuine weirdness did—reminded things how to be fully themselves.

Orvenna had been teaching him about direction this morning. How to notice when the resonance was starting. How to let it flow rather than forcing it. How to trust that his attention would find what needed waking without him micromanaging every moment. It was slower work than Sefiathan wanted. He kept expecting dramatic results, trees springing back to life, people suddenly discovering abilities. But Orvenna insisted the resonance was subtle. A reminder rather than a gift. A permission rather than a transformation.

"You're not fixing broken things," she'd said. "You're helping them remember they were never broken. Just sleeping. Just waiting for permission to be fully themselves."

The teaching had felt abstract until Calantha found him after lunch. She'd been at the gathering almost as long as Sefiathan now, one of the few humans who'd stayed through the whole spring. They'd become genuinely friendly over the past months—not the careful friendship where Sefiathan tracked every interaction for evidence of his worth, but the kind where they just enjoyed spending time together without performing for each other.

"Can I ask you something?" she said, sitting down beside him near the fire pit where he'd been trying to practice noticing the resonance in smaller things. Tracking how his attention moved through the flames, whether anything changed when he stopped controlling his focus.

"Yeah."

"Do you ever feel like people only tolerate you because you're useful?" The question came out careful but genuine. Not performative vulnerability but actual wondering.

Sefiathan looked at her. Calantha was maybe thirty, competent with fire magic in ways that made other wind-readers uncomfortable, quietly brilliant about thermal dynamics. She'd taught him the reed trick for starting fires weeks ago, had shared meals with him and Korrin and Tavis without seeming to keep score about who contributed what. He'd never gotten the sense she was performing friendship with him. But apparently she'd been worrying about whether she was performing it with everyone else.

"Yeah," he said. "I spent most of my life convinced people only kept me around because I was helpful. Like if I stopped being useful they'd realize I wasn't worth the effort."

"Did you ever figure out if that was true?"

"I figured out it was the wrong question." He poked at the fire with a stick, watching embers shift. "The real question was whether I could accept being loved without earning it. Whether I could just exist around people without constantly proving my worth."

Calantha was quiet for a moment. Then: "I think I'm in love with someone. But I keep trying to make it transactional. Keep trying to earn their affection by being helpful or impressive or whatever I think they want. And it's exhausting. But every time I try to just exist around them without performing, I panic. Like if I'm not constantly proving my worth they'll realize I'm not actually that interesting."

The recognition was so familiar it hurt. That was exactly what Sefiathan had been doing with everyone before coming to the Frostmere. Treating every relationship like a performance review. Every interaction like evidence for or against his worthiness. Every moment of genuine connection like something he had to earn rather than something that could just exist.

"Love is here to set you free," he said, the words coming out before he could analyze them. "Not trap you in obligation. Not turn you into a performing seal trying to earn affection. Just exist and trust that your genuine weirdness is exactly what they're interested in."

"That's terrifying."

"Yeah. Because it means accepting you might not be what they want. Means risking rejection without the safety net of thinking you just didn't perform correctly." He stopped poking the fire and looked at her directly. "But it also means if they do love you, it's actually you they love. Not the performance. Not the helpful version. Just the weird uncomfortable authentically you version."

Calantha absorbed this with the kind of stillness that meant she was actually thinking rather than just waiting to respond. "How do you stop making it transactional?"

"I'm still working on that. But I think it starts with noticing when you're performing. When you're trying to earn rather than just existing. And then stopping. Even though it feels terrifying. Even though every instinct says you need to keep proving your worth."

"What if they leave when I stop performing?"

"Then they weren't actually interested in you. They were interested in the performance. Which sucks but it's better to know that than spend your whole life trying to maintain an act."

Something shifted in Calantha's expression. Not dramatic but subtle—a kind of recognition settling into place. "I've been so focused on being what I thought they wanted that I haven't actually let them see who I am. I've been treating their interest like something I have to earn instead of something they're offering freely."

"Yeah. That's the trap." Sefiathan felt his own words landing with weight. Not because he'd figured this out perfectly but because he was still learning it himself. Still practicing letting people love him without earning it. Still working to accept that genuine connection didn't require perfect performance.

They sat together as the afternoon stretched into evening. Calantha didn't ask for advice about what to do next and Sefiathan didn't offer any. Just two people recognizing a shared pattern. Two people learning that love wasn't supposed to trap you in obligation but set you free to be authentically yourself. Even the weird parts. Especially the weird parts.

Later, when Calantha had gone to help with dinner preparation, Sefiathan found himself back at the dying tree. He didn't try to heal it or perform resonance or be authentically odd in strategic ways. Just sat with it. Just noticed that the small patch of healthier bark had grown slightly. Just let himself think uncomfortable thoughts about his family, about Mira, about all the relationships where he'd tried to earn love instead of accepting it freely.

The tree wasn't getting dramatically better. But it wasn't getting worse either. Something in it was remembering how to be healthy. Not because Sefiathan was forcing it or performing healing or trying to control the resonance. Just because he was genuinely present with his odd attention, not shrinking to make anyone comfortable, not performing for an imaginary audience.

Love setting you free meant accepting you didn't have to earn it. Meant trusting that genuine connection could exist without constant proof of worth. Meant letting yourself be authentically weird and uncomfortable and odd-angled without apologizing or performing or trying to make yourself palatable.

The people worth knowing would appreciate that. Would love you for your actual self rather than your performed self. Would offer affection freely rather than making you earn it through endless usefulness.

The evening cooled and the fire crackled and somewhere in the gathering Calantha was probably working up courage to stop performing for someone who might love her anyway. The tree beside Sefiathan was still mostly dying but there was a growing patch that remembered health and he was learning to accept love without transaction.
